## The mechanism (serves D3)

- **F1 [S-medium]:** LVT falls on the *unimproved* value of land — what the bare
  site is worth — not on the buildings on it; two identical neighbouring lots owe
  the same, whether one is empty and the other holds an apartment block. [LocalHousingSolutions; Sightline]
- **F2 [S-strong]:** Every NZ property already has an official **Land Value**
  figure — "the likely price that just the land (minus buildings and
  improvements) would sell for" — held separately from Capital Value in the
  council rating valuation. [QV; BullerDC]

## The efficiency reason (serves D1)

- **F3 [S-strong]:** Because land supply is fixed, a tax on land value causes
  little to no deadweight loss — owners can't make less land to dodge it — making
  it far more efficient than taxes on work or capital. *(Register cap: "far more
  efficient," not "costless" — real valuation is imperfect; see caveat C2.)* [ChicagoFed; IMF-WP-2022; NBER-w8913]
- **F4 [S-medium]:** Land rent is largely a *collectively created* value — it
  rises with location, public infrastructure and surrounding activity, not the
  owner's own effort — so taxing it returns community-made value to the community
  without blunting the incentive to build. [ChicagoFed; arXiv-2511.21766]
- **F5 [S-medium]:** Adam Smith already observed that a land value tax creates no
  deadweight loss and can't be passed on to tenants. [ChicagoFed]
- **F6 [S-medium]:** LVT discourages land banking: since the tax is owed whether
  or not a site is built on, holding vacant or under-used land becomes costly,
  pushing owners to develop or sell. [Sightline; LincolnInst; LocalHousingSolutions]

## The New Zealand fit (serves D4)

- **F7 [S-strong]:** NZ housing is among the least affordable in the developed
  world: June-2024-year median house price ≈ $753,500 vs median household
  disposable income ≈ $51,597 — about **14.6× income** nationally; Auckland has
  run ~8–11×. Demographia's "affordable" threshold is **3.0**. [StatsNZ; Demographia2024; interest.co.nz]
- **F8 [S-strong]:** NZ has **no comprehensive capital gains tax**; land gains
  are caught only by narrow rules like the bright-line test (a short window after
  purchase — 2 years as of 2024). [BDO; IRD; WK]
- **F9 [S-strong]:** The 2019 Tax Working Group noted a **land tax could be
  levied by councils as a local tax**; its broad-CGT recommendation was not
  adopted. NZ has implemented neither. [TWG-FinalReport; austaxpolicy]
- **F10 [S-strong]:** NZ councils already rate property and may legally set rates
  on **land value, capital value, or annual value**; many historically rated on
  land value, and valuations are refreshed at least every three years under the
  Rating Valuations Act 1998. *(This is what answers caveat C1 — the valuation
  machinery already exists and runs.)* [TeAra-Taxes; QV; BullerDC]
- **F11 [S-medium]:** The Henry George land-tax movement "had more effect in New
  Zealand than most places"; land-rating proposals appeared as early as 1849, and
  NZ levied a **national land tax until it was abolished in 1992**. [TeAra-EconThought; TeAra-Taxes; ExpatFocus]
- **F12 [S-medium]:** In 2024 **Wellington City Council was actively considering
  switching its rates to a land-value basis** — a live, present-day NZ debate. [Spinoff-Windbag]

## The strongest objection and the reply (serves D2)

- **F13 [S-medium]:** The strongest objection: LVT hits the **asset-rich but
  cash-poor** — retirees on fixed incomes, farmers whose wealth is locked in
  land — with a bill they can't pay from current income. *(Concede: this has
  genuine political force; see caveat C3.)* [LessWrong; TruthOnMarket]
- **F14 [S-strong]:** NZ already runs the fix: **rates postponement schemes**.
  Eligible owners (typically 65+, or low-income) can defer rates for life, repaid
  with interest only when the property is sold or settled from the estate. [Govt.nz-defer; QLDC; Napier; OAG-2006]
- **F15 [S-medium]:** Deferral for asset-rich, cash-poor owners is an established
  tool internationally (use-value and deferral programmes for farm/forest land),
  so the cash-flow problem is solvable by design. [Albemarle; Multnomah]

## Caveats the draft must honour (not new facts — handling rules)

- **C2:** "No deadweight loss" is the pure-theory limit; with imperfect real
  valuation, claim **"far more efficient,"** never "costless." (Tempers F3.)
- **C1:** Isolating unimproved land value is genuinely hard *in general* — but NZ
  already does it every three years (F2, F10), so the draft turns this objection
  into a strength rather than dodging it.
- **C3:** The cash-poor objection keeps political force even where deferral exists
  (uptake of relief is low, people dislike debt against the family home). The
  reply must concede this weight, not wave it away. [Retirement.govt.nz-research]
