# _template.md — how to write an audience profile

An audience profile tells the pipeline *who is reading and how to respect them.*
Six sections, all required. Copy this file, fill every section, save as
`<slug>.md` (lowercase, hyphenated). A filled example follows the blank schema —
a template plus a worked example is executable by a much dumber model than a
template alone.

The load contract: a run reads **exactly one** audience file. So each profile is
self-contained — no "see the other file."

---

## The schema

```
# <slug>.md — audience: <short name>

## 1. Who they are
One paragraph. The actual person, not a demographic cell.

## 2. What they want from a piece
Why they are reading. What makes them keep reading vs. bounce.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Reading level / sentence weather.
- What they ALREADY KNOW (the writer must never re-explain this).
- What counts as talking down to THEM specifically.

## 4. Imagery source domain
The world their metaphors come from (per imagery.md): the controlling
metaphor and supporting analogies should be drawable from here. Name 2–4
domains they live in.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
The specific question that gates every image and explanation: "would one of
them use this, among themselves?" Spell out what fails it here.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Tone notes. Things that instantly lose this reader. Anything a writer
unfamiliar with them would get wrong.
```

---

## Worked example (filled)

```
# trades.md — audience: working tradespeople

## 1. Who they are
Electricians, plumbers, chippies, sparkies — people who fix and build physical
things for a living and are sharp about systems, just not in academic language.
Time-poor, bullshit-allergic, proud of craft.

## 2. What they want from a piece
The point, fast, with the reasoning shown so they can check it — the same way
they'd want a job explained before they quote it. They bounce on throat-clearing
and on being lectured.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Plain, muscular sentences. Short is fine; vague is not.
- They ALREADY KNOW: how systems fail in the real world, how to read a spec,
  that the cheapest option usually isn't. Do not explain "trade-offs" to them.
- Talking down = explaining their own world back to them, or dressing a simple
  point in management-speak.

## 4. Imagery source domain
The job site and the toolbox; wiring, plumbing, load and structure; quoting and
warranties; weather and the working day.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
Would a sparky say this to another sparky on smoko? If the metaphor needs a
glossary, or sounds like a TED talk, it fails.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Earn trust by being concrete and admitting cost. Red lines: corporate
euphemism, fake mateyness, and any whiff of "let me explain how the world works
to you."
```
