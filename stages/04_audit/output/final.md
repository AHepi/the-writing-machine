# The Science of Can and Can't

<span class="subtitle">a from-zero crash course in constructor theory</span>

You do not need any mathematics or any physics to read this. You need only the
patience to meet a few new ideas one at a time. By the end you will be able to
say, in your own words, what constructor theory is; how it differs from the
ordinary way physics has worked for three hundred years; what its handful of core
ideas mean; what it has so far been used to talk about — information, life, and
heat; and — equally important — how much of it is settled and how much is still
an open bet by a small group of physicists. That last part matters, and I will
not hide it: this is a young idea, not a finished one.

We are going to do the whole thing in one kitchen. Recipes, cooks, ingredients,
and the dishes you can and can't make: that is the only picture you will need to
hold, and we will build everything else on top of it.

## Chapter 1 — A puzzle you already know

Crack an egg into a hot pan and stir. In a minute you have scrambled eggs. Now
try to run it backwards: take the scrambled eggs and turn them back into a whole,
unbroken egg with the yolk sitting neat inside the white. You can't. Nobody can.
Not a clumsy cook, not a brilliant one, not a cook with a billion-dollar
laboratory. The forward recipe is easy; the backward recipe is, as far as anyone
can tell, flatly impossible.

Here is a second one. Write a recipe on a card — *two eggs, a pinch of salt,
thirty seconds in the pan* — and you can copy that card perfectly, by hand, as
many times as you like. Every copy works as well as the original. But the cake
itself, the finished dish, never copies itself and never un-bakes. The *recipe*
copies freely; the *cake* does not.

So nature is fussy. It hands out some transformations for free and refuses others
outright, and the pattern of what it allows and what it forbids is not random —
it is lawful, dependable, the same in every kitchen on Earth. Step back and a
strange question appears, the question this whole book is about:

> **What if the deepest laws of physics were not predictions of what happens
> next, but statements about which transformations are possible and which are
> impossible — and why?**

That is the wager behind **constructor theory**. It was proposed by the physicist
David Deutsch in a 2013 paper, and it has been developed since then by Deutsch
together with Chiara Marletto at the University of Oxford.[^who] It is not a
crank's daydream and not settled textbook science either; it is a serious
research programme, made by serious people, that is still trying to prove its
worth. Marletto wrote a popular book about it with a title that captures the whole
idea in four words: *The Science of Can and Can't*.[^book]

Hold on to the egg and the recipe card. We will keep coming back to them, because
the egg you can't unscramble and the recipe you can copy turn out to be two of
the deepest facts in physics — and constructor theory is the attempt to put facts
of exactly that shape at the very bottom of the stack.

*Recap: nature permits some transformations and forbids others, lawfully.
Constructor theory asks whether those permissions and prohibitions — the can's
and can't's — should be the foundation of physics itself.*

## Chapter 2 — How physics normally works

To feel what is new about constructor theory, you have to first see clearly the
older picture it wants to join — the one physics has run on since Newton. Call it
the **prevailing conception**, because that is what it is: the standard, reigning
way of doing fundamental physics.

It works like this. Take any physical system — a planet, a billiard ball, a
galaxy, a single atom. Write down two things. First, its **initial conditions**:
where everything is right now and how fast it is moving. Second, its **laws of
motion**: the rules that say how those positions and speeds change from one
instant to the next. Feed both into the mathematics, turn the handle, and out
comes a prediction of what the system will do at every future moment.[^predict]

It is worth pausing on how staggeringly well this works. It is how we land probes
on comets, predict eclipses years ahead to the second, and design every bridge
and engine ever built. The prevailing conception is one of the most successful
ideas in human history. Constructor theory is not trying to throw it out. Keep
that firmly in mind: nobody is claiming Newton was wrong.

Think of the prevailing conception as a **cosmic prediction machine**. You give
it the present — the exact state of the kitchen, every ingredient in its place —
and it tells you the future, dish by dish, instant by instant. Brilliant. But
notice what kind of question it is built to answer. It answers *"given this
starting point, what happens next?"* It is a machine for grinding the present
forward into the future.

And here is the crack that constructor theory pushes into. Some of the most
interesting things in the world are awkward to phrase as "given this start, what
happens next?" Two of them in particular kept nagging at Deutsch: the physics of
**information** and the physics of **life**.[^strains] Think about a fact like
*"this message can be copied"* or *"this recipe can be passed on accurately."*
That is not really a statement about one particular system following one
particular trajectory from one particular starting point. It is a statement about
what is *possible* — about a whole sweep of would-be transformations, most of
which never actually happen. The prediction machine, for all its power, has no
natural slot for a claim like that. It tells you what *does* happen. It is oddly
quiet about what merely *could*.

That quiet is the opening. The next chapter walks through it.

*Recap: the prevailing conception predicts the future from initial conditions plus
laws of motion, and does so superbly — but it is built to say what happens, not
what is possible, and possibility is exactly where information and life live.*

## Chapter 3 — The science of can and can't

Here is the move. Constructor theory proposes that we state the laws of physics
**only** in terms of which transformations are possible and which are impossible,
and why.[^core] Not "given this start, here is the future," but "this kind of
change can be made happen; that kind never can."

A statement about what *could or could not* happen — rather than what actually
does — has a name: a **counterfactual**. The word means *contrary to fact*:
a claim about a state of affairs that may never come to pass. "I could have caught
the earlier train" is a counterfactual; you didn't, but you could have.
Constructor theory's central and most radical proposal is that counterfactuals
like these — *can* and *can't* — belong at the very foundation of physics, as
basic facts about the world, not as afterthoughts.[^counter] That is the whole
reason for the book title: it really is meant to be a *science of can and can't*.

If putting "can't" at the bottom of physics sounds exotic, here is the reassuring
part: physics has secretly done it for centuries. Take one of the most famous
laws there is, the conservation of energy — energy is never created or destroyed.
There is an exactly equivalent way to say the same thing, and it is a flat
statement of impossibility: **you cannot build a perpetual-motion machine.** You
cannot build the free-lunch kitchen that cooks forever out of nothing.[^perp]
That "cannot" is not a soft engineering caution; it is as hard as any law in
science. So the idea of a deep law that takes the form *"this is impossible"* is
not strange at all. Constructor theory's bet is that this shape — the impossible,
the forbidden recipe — is not the exception but the rule, the form *every*
fundamental law should take.

There is a lovely twist in this way of thinking, which Marletto puts crisply:
"Declaring something impossible leads to more things being possible."[^heur] It
sounds backwards, so here is the kitchen version. The moment you accept, as an
ironclad law, that no kitchen can cook from nothing, you suddenly know an enormous
amount about every real kitchen: every dish must be paid for in ingredients and
heat, every process must balance its books. A firm "can't" does not shrink the
world; it carves it into a definite shape, and a world with a definite shape is
one you can actually reason about. Rule out the impossible cleanly, and the
possible comes into focus.

The two ways of doing physics now sit side by side. It is worth seeing them in one
view:

| | The prevailing conception | Constructor theory |
|---|---|---|
| **What's fundamental** | initial conditions + laws of motion | which tasks are possible vs impossible |
| **What a law says** | "given this start, here is what happens next" | "this transformation can be made to happen; that one never can — and here's why" |
| **Its native question** | what *does* happen? | what *can* and *can't* happen? |
| **A familiar example** | predict a planet's orbit from its position and speed | a perpetual-motion machine is impossible |

*The same physics, two foundations: the standard picture makes the next moment
fundamental; constructor theory makes the possible/impossible divide fundamental
(F6, F7, F13, F16).*

The right-hand column is the whole programme in one box. Everything else is
working out how to speak that language precisely. For that, we need exactly three
words.

*Recap: constructor theory states laws as counterfactuals — what can and can't
happen — and that is less strange than it sounds, because conservation of energy
already is the impossibility of perpetual motion. Ruling out the impossible
sharpens what's possible.*

## Chapter 4 — The three words: task, constructor, substrate

To say "this transformation is possible" precisely, you need a word for the
transformation, a word for the thing that does it, and a word for the thing it is
done to. Three words. We already have perfect kitchen names for all three.

**A task is a recipe.** In constructor theory, a **task** is the abstract
specification of a transformation: *these* input attributes turned into *those*
output attributes.[^task] It is exactly what a recipe is — *raw eggs and salt go
in; scrambled eggs come out.* A task does not say who does it or how; it only says
what counts as having been done. It is the *what*, not the *how*. Notice it is a
specification, a description on a card, not an event: the recipe for a soufflé
exists whether or not anyone is cooking one tonight.

**A constructor is a cook.** A **constructor** is the thing that actually carries
the task out — and here is the one feature that makes the word do real work: a
constructor performs the task *and remains able to perform it again.*[^cons] A
cook scrambles your eggs and is still, a minute later, perfectly able to scramble
the next person's. An oven bakes one loaf and stands ready for the next. The cook
is not used up by the cooking. The technical comparison physicists reach for is a
**catalyst** — in chemistry, something that makes a reaction go while itself
emerging unchanged, ready to do it again.[^cat] A constructor is anything with
that catalyst-like quality: it drives a change and survives intact to drive it
once more.

**A substrate is the ingredients.** The **substrate** is the physical stuff the
constructor works on — the system whose attributes get transformed.[^sub] The
eggs are the substrate; the cook is the constructor; "scramble them" is the task.

With those three words, the central distinction of the whole theory becomes easy
to state. A task is **possible** if the laws of physics allow a constructor for it
to exist — even if nobody has built that constructor yet. A task is **impossible**
if a law of physics forbids it from ever being done with arbitrarily high
accuracy, no matter how clever the would-be cook.[^poss] "Scramble an egg" is a
possible task: cooks for it exist by the million. "Unscramble an egg" is, as far
as physics tells us, an impossible task: no cook for it can exist, ever. That is
the difference between the two recipes we started with in Chapter 1, now stated in
the theory's own terms.

Notice how much weight the word "possible" is carrying, because this is where the
new language earns its keep. In ordinary speech "possible" often means "someone
has done it." Here it means something stronger and stranger: a task is possible if
the laws of physics *permit a constructor for it*, whether or not anyone has ever
built one, or ever will. There are surely dishes no cook has yet invented — recipes
that have never once been made anywhere in the universe — which are nonetheless
perfectly possible, only waiting for a clever enough cook. The prediction machine
of Chapter 2 has nothing to say about a dish that has never been cooked; there is
no trajectory to compute, no event to predict. But constructor theory does have
something to say: it classes that uncooked dish firmly on the *possible* side of
the line, right alongside the egg you can scramble, and far away from the egg you
cannot unscramble. A law that can speak about transformations that have never
happened — and may never happen — is doing something the older picture
cannot.

That is genuinely all the machinery there is. Before we put it to work, let me
show you the single piece of notation in this entire book — and as promised, I
will name every symbol in it.

## Chapter 5 — Information as physics

Now the payoff begins. The reason Deutsch and Marletto wanted this language is
that it lets them talk cleanly about things the prediction machine handled
awkwardly — and the first of those is **information**.

What is information, physically? We have a strong everyday sense of it: a message,
a recipe card, the text of this book. The deep feature of information is that it
can be **copied**. You can take a recipe card and produce a second card that says
exactly the same thing, and then a third, each as good as the first. Now hear that
sentence in the theory's own words: *copying a recipe is a task, and it is a
possible task.* "Take a card bearing message X and produce a second card also
bearing X" — that is a transformation with an input and an output, and physics
permits a constructor for it. Information, in this view, *is* the kind of
attribute that certain copying-and-transforming tasks are possible for.

In 2015 Deutsch and Marletto published exactly this idea, in a paper called
"Constructor theory of information," in a leading physics journal.[^info] Its
boldest claim is a reversal of the usual order of things. We normally think of
information as a pure, abstract idea — something from mathematics or logic that
exists before and above the physical world, and that physical things merely
happen to carry. The constructor theory of information turns that around: it says
information is **not** an abstract given at all. What information is, and how it
behaves, is *fixed by the laws of physics* — specifically, by which copying and
transforming tasks those laws make possible or impossible.[^infolaw] Information
is physical to the bone.

That sounds abstract, so make it concrete in the kitchen again. Ask: *which
copying tasks does nature actually permit?* "Take a card reading **2 eggs, a pinch
of salt** and produce a second card reading the same" — permitted, easily; you do
it with a pencil. The whole edifice of writing, printing, and computer memory is
built on the fact that this particular task is possible. But you can equally well
ask about copying tasks that nature might *forbid*, and the surprising lesson of
modern physics is that not everything you can imagine copying can actually be
copied. The content of an information law lies exactly in that pattern — in the
sharp line between the copies you can make and the ones you can't. The constructor
theory of information is the attempt to write that line down as fundamental law,
rather than to assume, as we usually do, that information is some frictionless
ideal that physics merely stores.

Why go to this trouble? Because it offers a single, deep foundation for things
that otherwise sit in separate boxes. The everyday information in a recipe card
and the strange information inside a quantum computer look like utterly different
beasts. The hope of the constructor theory of information is that both are
species of one thing — both are patterns of which tasks are possible — and so
both can be understood with one set of laws.[^both] Whether that hope is fully
made good is another matter, and we will be honest about it in Chapter 8. But you
can already see the shape of the ambition: take the slippery notion of
information, and pin it to the floor of physics using nothing but the language of
can and can't.

*Recap: information is the kind of attribute that can be copied — and copying is a
possible task. The 2015 constructor theory of information argues information is not
an abstract idea floating above physics but something defined by the physical laws
of what can and can't be copied, with one root for classical and quantum
information alike.*

## Chapter 6 — Life without a designer

The second thing that nagged at Deutsch was **life** — and life is where the
kitchen metaphor reaches its most beautiful extension.

Living things look *designed*. An eye, a wing, a cell: each is so well-fitted to
its job that for most of history the only available explanation was a designer who
fitted it. Darwin's great achievement was to show how the appearance of design can
arise with no designer at all, through replication and natural selection: things
that copy themselves, with occasional small errors, and whose better-copying
versions come to outnumber the rest.[^darwin] That is not in dispute here. The
question Marletto asked is a *physicist's* question, one layer underneath Darwin:
**what must the laws of physics be like for that whole process to be possible in
the first place?**

In ordinary kitchen terms: life is not merely a recipe, and not merely a cook. Life is
a **recipe that builds the cook.** A living thing is like a recipe card which,
when followed, produces a brand-new cook *and* a fresh copy of the card — a cook
who can then go and do the same again. Run that loop and you have reproduction.
The astonishing claim of evolution is that this self-building loop needs no
designer to set it up.

Marletto's 2015 paper, "Constructor theory of life," asks whether such a loop is
compatible with what she calls **no-design laws** — laws of physics that contain
no special clauses for living things, no secret recipe for an eye hidden in the
fundamental equations.[^life] Her result is clean and, I think, genuinely
striking. Accurate self-reproduction, replication, and natural selection *are*
compatible with no-design laws — and the only non-trivial thing the laws must
allow is this: that **digital information** can be physically instantiated.[^nodesign]

"Digital" is the load-bearing word, so let me unpack it in the kitchen. A digital
recipe is one written in discrete, exact symbols — *2 eggs*, not *a generous
glug*; *200°C*, not *fairly hot* — so that it can be copied perfectly, with no
drift creeping in across thousands of copies. A vague, "analogue" recipe degrades
a little each time it is handed on by word of mouth, until the dish is
unrecognisable. A digital one does not.

You can feel the difference with a line of cooks passing a recipe down the bench.
Pass a soft, fuzzy instruction — *cook it till it looks about right* — from one cook
to the next, and twenty cooks later it is hopeless: each one rounds it off a
little, and the small errors pile up. But pass on a single sharp instruction —
*exactly two eggs* — and twenty cooks later it is still *exactly two eggs*: a choice
between distinct, countable symbols leaves no room for the errors to accumulate.
That is the entire magic of "digital," and it is why a recipe written in exact
symbols can be copied ten thousand times without drifting, while a recipe shouted
across a noisy kitchen cannot survive ten retellings. Life needs that drift-free
exactness, and life has it: this is what the genetic code, written in the discrete
letters of DNA, actually provides. The
deep point is that the *possibility of exact, digital copying* is the one special
gift the laws of physics must offer for open-ended life to get going. Grant that
one thing, and no designer is required for any of the rest.

The paper adds one more piece worth knowing, because it sharpens the picture. A
mere **replicator** — the bare recipe card — is not enough on its own. To be
copied accurately, a replicator needs a **vehicle**: the surrounding machinery
that reads it and carries the copying out. The recipe card plus the cook-and-
kitchen that reads it and rebuilds it: together those two form a
**self-reproducer**.[^vehicle] In a living cell, the DNA is the replicator and the
rest of the cell's machinery is the vehicle. Neither copies itself alone; together
they close the loop.

*Recap: living things look designed but aren't. Constructor theory of life shows
that self-reproduction and natural selection are compatible with laws that have no
special provisions for life, provided one thing — that the laws allow exact,
digital information to be copied. A replicator needs a vehicle; together they make
a self-reproducer.*

## Chapter 7 — The one-way kitchen: thermodynamics

Return to the egg you can't unscramble, because it is time to give that
stubbornness its proper name. The study of heat, energy, and which way processes
naturally run is called **thermodynamics**, and its most famous result is the
**second law of thermodynamics**: left alone, things run *down* and not back up.
Heat flows from hot to cold, never the reverse; milk stirs into coffee but never
out of it; the egg scrambles but never unscrambles. The kitchen, almost entirely,
is a **one-way kitchen.**

You can already hear that this is a statement about can and can't. The second law,
spelled out, says: *the task of running this transformation forward is possible;
the task of running it backward is impossible.* It is a law about which recipes
nature will and will not let you cook. That is why thermodynamics fits the
constructor-theory mould so naturally — it was practically *born* speaking the
language of impossibility.[^thermo] Recall the deepest example from Chapter 3:
energy conservation is precisely the impossibility of a perpetual-motion machine
(F16). Thermodynamics is full of "can't"s like that, and constructor theory's job
is to state them exactly.

That precision is the actual contribution. The traditional statements of the
second law come with fine print — they tend to assume large systems, or
near-equilibrium conditions, or averages over many particles, and they get
slippery when you ask about a single small machine. Marletto's "Constructor Theory
of Thermodynamics" aims to give the second law an *exact*, scale-independent
formulation in the language of possible and impossible tasks — one that holds for
a single tiny constructor as cleanly as for a steam engine.[^thermolaw] Whether it
fully succeeds is, again, part of the open question of Chapter 8; the point for
now is that the *kind* of law thermodynamics is — a law of one-way recipes — is
exactly the kind constructor theory was built to express.

The same treatment has been extended even to **probability** — the mathematics of
chance and uncertainty — in a paper called "Constructor Theory of
Probability."[^prob] The details are beyond a first crash course, but the
direction is the one you would now predict: take a notion usually imported from
pure mathematics, and try to ground it in physical statements about which tasks
are possible.

*Recap: the second law of thermodynamics says forward transformations are possible
and their reverses are impossible — a law of one-way recipes, naturally a
can't-statement. Constructor theory aims to state it exactly, at any scale, and
extends the same approach to probability.*

## Chapter 8 — Is it true? What's settled, and what's open

I have saved the most important chapter for last, and I have built it at the same
density as the first, because the easiest way to mislead you about a young idea is
to let the excitement run on past the evidence. So here is the honest accounting.

First, what is genuinely real and not in doubt. The *papers exist* and have
passed peer review in respectable journals: the foundational 2013 paper, the
constructor theory of information (2015), the constructor theory of life (2015),
and the work on thermodynamics and probability. These are real proposals by real
physicists, worked out in mathematical detail. We can lay out plainly what has
been built so far:

| Subsidiary theory | Everyday handle | The core claim |
|---|---|---|
| **Information** (2015) | a recipe you can copy exactly | information is defined by which copying tasks physics permits, not by abstract logic |
| **Life** (2015) | a recipe that builds the cook | self-reproduction needs no designer, only laws that allow digital information |
| **Thermodynamics** | the one-way kitchen | the second law stated exactly as which tasks are possible vs impossible |
| **Probability** | — | chance grounded in physical possibility rather than pure mathematics |

*The same can/can't language has been applied, in published work, to information,
life, and thermodynamics (F17, F19, F20, F22, F24, F25, F26).*

Now, the part that intellectual honesty demands. Constructor theory is a **young
research programme, not established physics.** It is not in the standard textbooks.
Most working physicists do not use it, and many have not formed a view on it at
all. It sits outside the current mainstream, and it has not been confirmed by
experiment.[^status] Even sympathetic coverage of the programme says the quiet
part out loud: whether constructor theory "can really deliver … remains to be
seen," and broad experimental backing is, so far, not there.[^remains] When
you explain this idea to someone, that sentence belongs in your explanation. It is
not a defeat of the idea; it is the accurate status of the idea.

So is there *any* point where this style of thinking touches an experiment you
could actually run? The most concrete one is striking, and it is worth ending on
— with a careful fence around it. Marletto, working with the physicist Vlatko
Vedral, proposed a real test concerning **gravity**.[^grav] The reasoning runs
like this. Take two small masses and place each in a **superposition** — a genuine
quantum situation in which a particle has no single definite position but is, in a
precise sense, spread across more than one place at once. Let the two masses
interact through nothing but their mutual gravity. If, through gravity alone, they
become **entangled** — woven into a shared quantum state so that you cannot fully
describe one without the other — then, Marletto and Vedral argue, gravity itself
must be quantum. The crux is a clean impossibility statement of exactly the kind
this book has been about: a purely *classical* go-between cannot create
entanglement between two systems.[^witness] So if gravity manages it, gravity is
not classical.

This test is regarded as far closer to feasible than the older dreams of quantum
gravity, such as detecting an individual graviton — though "closer" still means
fiercely difficult, and it has not yet been carried out.[^feasible] And here is the
fence: this experiment is *inspired by* the same counterfactual, information-first
style of reasoning that Marletto pursues, but it is **not** a direct test of
constructor theory as a whole. If it succeeds, it tells us something profound about
gravity; it does not, by itself, certify the larger programme. I draw the line
there deliberately, because blurring it is exactly how a young idea gets oversold.

Step back and you can see why someone like Deutsch finds the whole project worth a
career. Long before constructor theory, in his 1997 book *The Fabric of Reality*,
Deutsch argued that four great strands of explanation — quantum physics,
evolution, computation, and the theory of knowledge — are far more deeply
connected than they look, and that you cannot fully understand any one of them
without the others.[^fabric] Constructor theory is, in a sense, the tool he and
Marletto built to try to make good on that vision: a single language — possible
and impossible tasks — general enough to talk about information, life, heat, and
ordinary matter all at once. That is the dream. Whether the dream becomes physics
is precisely what is still being decided.

*Recap and close: the papers are real and peer-reviewed, but constructor theory is
young, outside the mainstream, and not experimentally confirmed — say so when you
explain it. Its most concrete contact with experiment, the gravity-entanglement
test, is a related idea, not a verdict on the whole. The ambition is a single
language of can and can't deep enough to unify information, life, and matter — and
that ambition is still an open bet. You can now explain all of that in your own
words, which is exactly what we set out to do.*

## Glossary

- **Task** — the specification of a transformation: a given input attribute turned
  into a given output attribute. A recipe. (Notation: $a \rightarrow b$, "turn
  *a* into *b*.")
- **Constructor** — something that carries out a task and remains able to carry it
  out again, like a catalyst. A cook, or an oven.
- **Substrate** — the physical system a constructor acts on; the thing being
  transformed. The ingredients.
- **Possible task** — a task that the laws of physics allow a constructor for, even
  if none has been built yet.
- **Impossible task** — a task that some law of physics forbids from ever being
  done with arbitrarily high accuracy.
- **Counterfactual** — a statement about what could or could not happen, as opposed
  to what actually does happen.
- **Prevailing conception** — the standard way of doing physics: predict the future
  from initial conditions plus laws of motion.
- **No-design laws** — laws of physics with no special provisions for living
  things; no design built into the fundamental rules.
- **Digital information** — information written in discrete, exact symbols, so it
  can be copied perfectly without drifting; the kind life relies on.
- **Replicator** — the thing that gets copied, on its own (e.g. a recipe card, or
  DNA).
- **Vehicle** — the surrounding machinery that reads a replicator and carries out
  the copying.
- **Self-reproducer** — a replicator together with its vehicle, forming a system
  that copies itself.
- **Second law of thermodynamics** — the law that, left alone, processes run one
  way and not the reverse (heat flows hot to cold; eggs scramble but don't
  unscramble).
- **Superposition** — a genuine quantum situation in which a system has no single
  definite value (e.g. position) but is spread across more than one at once.
- **Entanglement** — a shared quantum state of two systems so linked that neither
  can be fully described on its own.

## Further reading

- **Chiara Marletto, *The Science of Can and Can't* (2021)** — the popular book by
  one of the programme's two principals; the friendliest full-length way in.
  [S4]
- **"How to Rewrite the Laws of Physics in the Language of Impossibility,"
  *Quanta Magazine* (2021)** — an excellent, careful magazine explainer with
  Marletto, including the honest caveats. [S6]
- **David Deutsch, "Constructor Theory," *Synthese* (2013)** — the foundational
  paper; technical, but this is where it all starts. [S1]
- **Deutsch & Marletto, "Constructor theory of information," *Proc. R. Soc. A*
  (2015)** — the information paper. [S7]
- **Chiara Marletto, "Constructor theory of life," *J. R. Soc. Interface*
  (2015)** — the life paper. [S11]
- **constructortheory.org** — the programme's own site, with copies of the papers
  and newer work. [S17]
- **David Deutsch, *The Fabric of Reality* (1997)** — the earlier book whose
  vision of four interwoven "strands" sets the stage for the whole project. [S10]

[^who]: David Deutsch's foundational paper "Constructor Theory" appeared in the
journal *Synthese* in 2013; he and Chiara Marletto have developed the programme at
the University of Oxford since around 2012 (F1, F2).

[^book]: Marletto's *The Science of Can and Can't* was published in 2021 (F3).

[^predict]: This is the "prevailing conception" of fundamental physics: predict
what will happen from a system's initial conditions plus its laws of motion (F6).

[^strains]: A stated motivation for constructor theory is that the physics of
information and of life are awkward to capture in the language of trajectories and
initial conditions (F8).

[^core]: Constructor theory expresses physical laws exclusively in terms of which
transformations ("tasks") are possible versus impossible, and why (F7).

[^counter]: Constructor theory deliberately admits counterfactual statements —
about what could or could not happen — into fundamental physics (F14, F15).

[^perp]: Conservation of energy is equivalent to the statement that a
perpetual-motion machine is impossible — physics already hides can't-statements at
its foundations (F16).

[^heur]: Marletto's phrasing of a guiding heuristic of the programme (F9).

[^task]: A task is the abstract specification of a transformation as input–output
pairs of attributes (F10).

[^cons]: A constructor is an entity that can cause a task to occur while retaining
the ability to cause it again (F11).

[^cat]: The catalyst comparison is the one Marletto uses: something that drives a
change while remaining able to do it again (F11).

[^sub]: A substrate is the physical system a constructor acts on, whose attributes
are transformed (F12).

[^poss]: A task is impossible if a law of physics forbids its being done with
arbitrarily high accuracy; otherwise it is possible — a constructor for it could in
principle exist (F13).

[^info]: Deutsch & Marletto, "Constructor theory of information," *Proceedings of
the Royal Society A* (2015) (F17).

[^infolaw]: The paper holds that information is not an a priori mathematical or
logical concept; its nature is fixed by the laws of physics — by which
transformations are possible or impossible (F18).

[^both]: The programme aims for constructor-theoretic laws underlying both
classical and quantum information (F19).

[^darwin]: Neo-Darwinian evolution explains the appearance of design without a
designer, via replication and natural selection (F21).

[^life]: Marletto, "Constructor theory of life," *Journal of the Royal Society
Interface* (2015) (F20).

[^nodesign]: Its central result: accurate self-reproduction, replication and
natural selection are compatible with no-design laws, the only non-trivial
requirement being that the laws allow digital information to be physically
instantiated (F22).

[^vehicle]: Under no-design laws an accurate replicator requires a vehicle that,
together with the replicator, constitutes a self-reproducer (F23).

[^thermo]: Thermodynamics fits the can/can't mould: some transformations run one
way but not the reverse — the second law — stated as which tasks are possible vs
impossible (F25).

[^thermolaw]: Marletto's "Constructor Theory of Thermodynamics" aims to state the
second law exactly and scale-independently in constructor-theoretic terms (F24).

[^prob]: Marletto's "Constructor Theory of Probability" extends the approach to
probabilistic statements (F26).

[^status]: Constructor theory is an early-stage research programme, not
established or experimentally confirmed, and outside the mainstream (F27).

[^remains]: Even sympathetic coverage notes that whether constructor theory can
"really deliver … remains to be seen," with broad empirical validation still
undeveloped (F28).

[^grav]: Marletto & Vedral (2017, *Physical Review Letters*) proposed that
gravitationally induced entanglement between two masses would be evidence that
gravity is quantum (F29).

[^witness]: The crux is that a purely classical mediator cannot create
entanglement between two systems — so if gravity does, gravity is not classical
(F29).

[^feasible]: This test is considered closer to feasible than detecting gravitons or
Planck-scale effects, but remains very hard and has not yet been performed (F30).

[^fabric]: In *The Fabric of Reality* (1997) Deutsch argued that quantum physics,
evolution, computation, and the theory of knowledge are deeply interconnected
(F4, F5).
