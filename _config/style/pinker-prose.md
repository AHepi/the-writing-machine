# pinker-prose.md — the delivery floor (full)

*The prose standard every draft must clear, after Steven Pinker's account of
**classic style** in* The Sense of Style*. This is the single prose floor — every
stage that writes loads this file in full. Nothing here is optional flair — it is
the floor a draft stands on.*

---

## The governing stance: classic style

Classic style models prose as a **window onto the world**. The writer has seen
something the reader hasn't yet, and directs the reader's gaze toward it. The
prose itself should be invisible; the reader should come away thinking about the
subject, not the sentences.

Three consequences follow, and they do most of the work:

1. **The reader is a competent equal**, not a student to be lectured or a mark
   to be managed. You are showing a peer something they happen not to have seen.
   This is the root of the respect floor in `rules.md`.
2. **Truth is presentable.** Classic style assumes the world is intelligible and
   the writer's job is to show it clearly — not to hedge, qualify, and
   self-protect on every line.
3. **The prose is about the world, not about itself.** Cut sentences that
   comment on the writing ("In this essay I will argue…", "It is interesting to
   note…"). Show the thing.

## The rules of the floor

**Concrete over abstract.** Prefer things you can see, count, and touch to
nominalizations and category-words. "The cost fell by half" beats "a reduction
in the cost metric was observed." Zombie nouns (*-tion, -ment, -ity*) kill
prose; turn them back into verbs.

**Show, don't signpost.** Drop metadiscourse — the throat-clearing that
announces structure instead of delivering content. The argument's shape should
be felt through the actual sentences, not narrated ("First, I will… Second, I
will…").

**Cut the hedges.** *Somewhat, fairly, rather, in a sense, arguably, it could be
argued.* A pile of qualifiers is not rigor; it is anxiety. Make the claim, or
make a smaller claim you can stand behind.

**No professional narcissism.** The reader does not care about your field's
internal disputes, your methodological throat-clearing, or how hard the topic
was to research. Spend the reader's attention on the world.

**The curse of knowledge is the chief enemy.** You know too much; the reader
knows less, and you cannot feel the gap. Name the thing before you abbreviate
it. Give an example before the abstraction. Read it as someone meeting the idea
for the first time. (But — see `rules.md` respect floor — "first time with the
*topic*" is not "first time *thinking*." Don't over-correct into condescension.)

**Coherence through the reader's eyes.** Each sentence should pick up a thread
the last one put down. Given-before-new: start a sentence with what the reader
already knows, end it with what's new. Arcs, not lists.

**Prose rhythm.** Vary sentence length. A short sentence lands a point. A longer
one, with its clauses unfolding in order, can carry a process or a qualification
that a short one would cram. End sentences and paragraphs on the strong word.

## What classic style is *not*

- Not plain style (which distrusts all metaphor and ornament) — classic style
  uses imagery freely, as a window can hold a vivid scene. See
  `_config/style/imagery.md`.
- Not oracular or postmodern style (prose about prose, the writer performing
  difficulty).
- Not relentless simplicity — a complex idea gets the structure it needs; the
  complexity is in the *idea*, never in the *prose covering it up*.

## The test

Read the draft as the world, not as text. If a sentence makes you think about
the writing — its caution, its cleverness, its scaffolding — it has failed the
floor. Rewrite until the window is clean.
