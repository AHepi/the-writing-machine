# child.md — the bright child (roughly 8–11)

*Starter profile. The respect floor matters most here: a child is new to almost
every topic but is a ferociously active thinker. Write up to them, never down.*

## 1. Who they are

A curious kid who asks "but why?" three times in a row and catches you when your
answer cheats. Limited vocabulary, unlimited reasoning appetite. Bored instantly
by anything that talks to them like a baby.

## 2. What they want from a piece

A real answer to a real question, told as something that actually happens in the
world. Concrete, a little funny, with a picture in their head on every page.
"Worth it" = they go tell someone the cool thing they just learned.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge

- **Reading level:** short sentences, common words, one idea at a time. Name a
  hard word once, plainly, then use it.
- **Already knows (do NOT re-explain):** how the playground works — fairness,
  taking turns, that pushing has consequences, that some kids are bigger; how
  games have rules; that things fall down; basic sharing and trading.
- **Talking down to them =** baby voice, fake enthusiasm ("Wow, isn't science
  AMAZING?!"), moralising, or pretending a question is silly. Kids smell
  condescension instantly.

## 4. Their world (for imagery)

Playground physics — pushing, sliding, swinging, balance; games and their rules;
sharing snacks and trading cards; pets; weather; building with blocks. The
controlling metaphor comes from here.

## 5. Heat and humour

Silliness lands hard; sarcasm doesn't (it reads as mean). Gross facts and big
numbers delight. No "provocation" in the political sense — heat here means
wonder and surprise.

## 6. Lines not to cross

- Never moralise or lecture.
- Never fake-simplify into something untrue — kids hate being lied to more than
  they hate being confused.
- Don't assume they can't follow a real idea; assume they can't follow a long
  sentence.
