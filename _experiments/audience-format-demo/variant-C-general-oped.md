# Wellington Is Right to Eye a Land Tax. The Whole Country Should.

When Wellington City Council floated the idea, in 2024, of shifting its rates onto
the value of land, the reaction was the usual mix of alarm and shrugs. It deserved
neither. It was the most sensible tax idea a New Zealand council has put on the
table in years, and the only odd thing about it is that the rest of the country
isn't following.

Here is the case, and I'll put my conclusion first: New Zealand is almost the
perfect country for a land value tax, and we are one of the few places on earth
that could bring one in tomorrow without building a single new piece of machinery.

Start with what such a tax actually touches, because the name oversells the pain.
A land value tax falls on the bare ground under a property — what the empty section
alone would sell for — and leaves the house on top alone. Picture two identical
sites: one holds an apartment block, the other a weedy vacant lot a speculator is
sitting on, waiting for the price to climb. Under this tax they pay exactly the
same, because the land beneath them is worth the same. Build something, and you owe
nothing extra. Hoard an empty section while the city begs for housing, and the bill
finds you.

Why land, of all things? Because it is the one thing that can't run away. Tax
income too hard and people work a little less or get paid offshore. Tax a company
and it shifts its profits to a friendlier country. But a quarter-acre in Mount Eden
will be a quarter-acre in Mount Eden whatever the rate — you can tax it and it
stays put, doing exactly what it did. So a land tax raises money without the waste
that chases the others around. This isn't a fringe theory; Adam Smith pointed it
out, and it follows from the simplest fact about land: nobody is making any more
of it.

And it's fair, which is the rarer thing. Think of a house whose section jumped in
value this year because a train station opened two streets over. The owner did
nothing — no new room, no fresh paint. The town made that gain — the station, the
school, the café on the corner. A land value tax simply asks for a
slice of the value the community created back from the person lucky enough to be
standing on it. Your house is your effort, and the tax leaves it alone. The land's
rising worth is the town's gift, and the tax takes a little of the gift.

New Zealand needs this more than most. We are nearly alone in the rich world in
having no broad capital gains tax, so money that would elsewhere spread across many
investments has piled into the one asset the taxman barely sees: land. We turned
housing into the national savings account, and the price proved it — by 2024 the
median house cost around 14.6 times the median household income, against an
international "affordable" mark of three. A tax that falls hardest on land, and on
land held idle, aims straight at the thing that has run away from us.

The usual objection to a land tax, everywhere else, is that you can't value the
land apart from the building — too hard, too slippery. In New Zealand that excuse
collapses. Councils already rate property and may legally rate on land value;
Quotable Value already revalues the land under every property on a three-year
cycle; we ran a national land tax until 1992. The meter is already on the wall. We
aren't being asked to build a machine — only to turn one we already own.

Now the objection that actually deserves respect, because it is the one that keeps
sinking land taxes at the ballot box. Picture the widow in the villa she bought in
1975, now worth two million through no act of hers, living on the pension. A tax on
her land's soaring value is a bill she simply cannot pay. Tax her on wealth she
can't spend and you can tax her out of her home — a real cost, and pretending
otherwise is how you lose the argument.

But New Zealand already built the answer. Councils run rates postponement schemes:
an eligible owner — typically over sixty-five or on a low income — can stop paying
and let the bill accrue against the property, repaid only when it's sold or settled
from the estate. Apply that to a land tax and the widow keeps her villa. She pays
nothing from her pension; the tax comes out of the windfall at the moment the
windfall becomes real money. The catch is honest: take-up of these schemes is low,
and people hate debt stacking up against the family home, so the relief would have
to be generous and close to automatic. That's a problem of design — and design is
fixable.

So here is what's actually on the table. A tax almost nobody can dodge. A tax cheap
to run, on rails we already own. A tax that gently punishes sitting on an empty
section and rewards the person who builds. Wellington saw it. The corner got lucky
because the town made it lucky — and it's long past time the lucky corner paid a
little of that luck back.
