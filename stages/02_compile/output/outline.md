# outline.md — constructor theory crash course

*The beats in order, each naming its F-tags, then the mandatory `## Imagery`
block and the per-beat visual marks. Stage 03 renders exactly this — nothing off
the plan. Format: textbook; audience: beginner; provocation: none; visuals: auto.*

## Beats

**B0 — Title block (D1).** Title, one-line subtitle, and a short "what you'll be
able to do by the end" promise. No facts.

**B1 — Chapter 1: the puzzle and the question (D2).** Open on an everyday puzzle:
you can scramble an egg but never unscramble it; you can copy a recipe word for
word but a cake never un-bakes. Nature plainly allows some changes and forbids
others. Name the governing question the whole course answers: *what if the
deepest laws of physics were statements about what is possible and impossible,
rather than predictions of what happens next?* Sets up that this is a real
proposal by working physicists (F1, F2). Recap.

**B2 — Chapter 2: how physics normally works (D3).** The prevailing conception:
give a system its starting state and its laws of motion, and physics grinds out
what happens next — a kind of cosmic prediction machine (F6). What it is
magnificent at; where it strains — information and life don't reduce neatly to
trajectories (F8). Context: this is the picture constructor theory wants to sit
*beneath*, not delete. Recap.

**B3 — Chapter 3: the science of can and can't (D4, D6).** The reframing: laws as
which tasks are possible vs impossible, and why (F7). What a counterfactual is —
a claim about what could/couldn't happen, not what does (F14, F15). Show the idea
isn't alien: energy conservation already *is* the impossibility of a
perpetual-motion machine (F16). The heuristic that ruling things out opens things
up (F9). *Visual mark: TABLE comparing the two pictures.* Recap.

**B4 — Chapter 4: the three words — task, constructor, substrate (D5).** Define
task (F10), constructor (F11), substrate (F12) in plain words; the catalyst image
for "does it and can do it again" (F11). Define possible/impossible task (F13). A
**"math, gently" box** introduces the only notation in the book: writing a task as
input attributes → output attributes, naming every symbol. *Visual mark: DIAGRAM
of a constructor acting on a substrate.* Recap.

**B5 — Chapter 5: information as physics (D7).** The first place the new language
pays off: treat information by asking which copying/transforming tasks are
possible (F18, F19). The 2015 information paper (F17). Why "information is
physical" matters and how classical and quantum information might share one root
(F19). Recap.

**B6 — Chapter 6: life without a designer (D7).** Living things look designed but
aren't (F21). The constructor theory of life (F20) shows self-reproduction,
replication and selection are compatible with no-design laws, *if* the laws let
digital information be instantiated (F22). The replicator-plus-vehicle =
self-reproducer result (F23). Recap.

**B7 — Chapter 7: the one-way kitchen — thermodynamics (D7).** Some
transformations go one way only; that one-wayness is the second law, naturally a
statement of which tasks are impossible (F25). The constructor theory of
thermodynamics aims to state it exactly (F24). Tie back to perpetual motion
(F16). Brief note that probability gets the same treatment (F26). Recap.

**B8 — Chapter 8: is it true? what's settled, what's open (D8).** Honest status:
a young programme, not established or experimentally confirmed, outside the
mainstream (F27, F28). The most concrete contact with experiment is the
Marletto–Vedral gravity-entanglement proposal (F29, F30) — framed as the broader
research direction touching experiment, not as confirmation of constructor theory
itself. Place the work in Deutsch's lifelong project of unifying physics,
computation, life and knowledge (F4, F5). *Visual mark: TABLE summarizing the
subsidiary theories built so far.* Recap that doubles as the book's close: what
the reader can now say, and what honest people are still arguing about.

**B9 — Glossary (D9).** One line each for: task, constructor, substrate,
possible/impossible task, counterfactual, prevailing conception, no-design laws,
digital information, replicator, vehicle, self-reproducer, the second law,
entanglement, superposition.

**B10 — Further reading (D10).** Annotated, pointing at real sources: Marletto's
book (S4), the Quanta explainer (S6), the foundational paper (S1), the
information / life papers (S7, S11), constructortheory.org (S17).

*Length target: 6,000–9,000 words (D12). Every chapter carries a hook, a worked
kitchen analogy, plain definitions, and a recap — the last chapter at the density
of the first (D11).*

## Imagery

**Controlling metaphor: the kitchen — recipes, cooks, and ingredients.** Drawn
straight from the beginner's world (`beginner.md` §4: kitchens and recipes). It
is introduced at its simplest in Chapter 1 and *extended*, never swapped, as the
ideas deepen.

- **Compatible domain (the whole book stays inside it):** cooking and the
  kitchen — recipes, cooks, ingredients, dishes, kitchen tools/appliances, the
  one-way transformations of cooking, copying a recipe card.

Mapped images, each to its beat:

- **Recipe = task** (B1, B4): a recipe is a specification — *these* ingredients
  in, *that* dish out — exactly what a task is (F10). Simplest form, introduced
  early.
- **Cook / appliance = constructor** (B4): the cook (or the oven) carries out the
  recipe and is still able to make it again tomorrow — the "does it and can do it
  again" of a constructor (F11). A reusable kitchen tool is the catalyst image.
- **Ingredients = substrate** (B4): the stuff being transformed (F12).
- **Impossible recipe = impossible task** (B1, B3, B7): a dish no cook however
  skilled could ever make because nature forbids it — e.g. a recipe to *unscramble*
  an egg (F13, F25). The unscrambling example opens the book (B1) and returns for
  thermodynamics (B7): same image, extended.
- **The free-lunch recipe = perpetual motion** (B3, B7): a kitchen that cooks
  forever out of nothing — the forbidden recipe behind energy conservation (F16).
- **Copyable recipe card = information** (B5): a recipe written so exactly that
  another cook can copy it perfectly and make the identical dish — physical
  information, and why "digital" (exact, discrete) copying matters (F18, F22).
- **A recipe that builds the cook = life / self-reproducer** (B6): a recipe that,
  when followed, produces a new cook *and* a fresh copy of the recipe — the
  replicator (the recipe card) plus its vehicle (the cook/kitchen that reads and
  rebuilds it) forming a self-reproducer (F22, F23).
- **One-way recipes = the second law** (B7): you can stir milk into coffee but
  never stir it back out; cooking mostly runs one way (F25).

**Condescension test:** beginners reach for kitchen and recipe analogies among
themselves constantly — this is their own register, not an outsider's caricature.
Passes. No memes (textbook format: memes off).
