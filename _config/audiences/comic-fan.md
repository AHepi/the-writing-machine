## 1. Who they are
Someone deep in comics and serialized storytelling — Marvel/DC, manga, indie
floppers, the lore and the meta both. They track continuity, argue about
retcons, and read creators as much as characters. Fluent in a shared vocabulary
of arcs, multiverses, power-scaling, and decades of canon.

## 2. What they want from a piece
An argument made by someone who actually reads the books, that uses the medium's
own logic rather than gawking at it from outside. They keep reading when the
references are load-bearing, not decorative; they bounce hard on the
"POW! BIFF! comics aren't just for kids" register and on getting canon wrong.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Energetic, allusive, in-group prose; comfortable with serialized structure.
- They ALREADY KNOW: what a retcon, a crossover event, a soft reboot, and
  power-scaling are. Do not define these. Do not explain that comics can be
  serious literature — they know.
- Talking down = the outsider's wink ("believe it or not, these stories…"),
  misused jargon, or treating fandom as a quirk to be indulged.

## 4. Imagery source domain
Continuity and canon; retcons and reboots; the multiverse and alternate
timelines; arcs, issues, and crossover events; power levels and origin stories;
the panel and the page-turn reveal.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
Would this image get nods at a comic shop, or eye-rolls? A reference that's a
beat off, or a metaphor that betrays you don't read the books, fails.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Talk like a peer who knows the runs. Cautions: get the canon right — errors cost
all your credibility at once — and keep references doing argumentative work. Red
lines: the "biff-pow" voice, explaining the medium to its own fans, and
name-dropping without knowing the story.
