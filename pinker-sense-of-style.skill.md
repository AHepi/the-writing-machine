# Writing with a Sense of Style — Pinker, made operational

This card teaches you to write the way Steven Pinker argues good writers actually
write. It is organized around the book's real architecture: **classic style**
(the stance), **the curse of knowledge** (the chief enemy), **syntax** (the web,
the tree, the string), **coherence** (arcs of coherence), and **usage** (telling
real rules from myths). Read it to *understand the model of writing*, then apply
the checklists.

> Pinker's root conviction: bad writing is rarely caused by laziness or stupidity.
> It is caused by writers who can't see their prose the way a reader will. Almost
> everything below is a technique for escaping your own head and serving a reader
> who knows less than you and can't read your mind.

**The two pillars, in one breath.** Write as if you are *showing a competent
equal something worth seeing in the world* (classic style), and assume that
*you cannot feel what your reader doesn't yet know* (the curse of knowledge), so
you must check rather than trust your sense of clarity.

---

## 1. Classic style — the master stance

Most bad prose comes from adopting the wrong *stance* toward the reader without
realizing you've adopted one at all. Pinker (following Thomas & Turner's *Clear
and Simple as the Truth*) recommends **classic style** as your default.

### The governing metaphor: prose is a window onto the world

> "The writer can see something that the reader has not yet noticed, and he
> orients the reader's gaze so that she can see it for herself."

The prose itself is a pane of glass. The reader should look *through* the words at
the thing being shown, never *at* the words. When a reader notices your prose —
its caution, its cleverness, its scaffolding — the window has smudged.

### The postulates of classic style (internalize these — they generate everything else)

1. **Asymmetry of sight, not of status.** You have seen something the reader
   hasn't *yet*. That is the only asymmetry. The reader is otherwise your equal —
   as smart as you, just not yet looking where you're looking.
2. **The aim is to direct a gaze.** Writing is *showing*, not arguing-at,
   lecturing, or managing. You point; the reader looks and sees for themselves.
3. **The model scene is conversation between equals.** Write as if speaking to one
   intelligent person about a shared subject. This single mental image fixes tone,
   diction, and the right amount of explanation.
4. **Truth is knowable and presentable.** Classic style *presumes* the world is
   intelligible and your job is to show it clearly — not to hedge, qualify, and
   self-protect on every line. Clarity is treated as the proof that you've seen
   truly.
5. **Effort is concealed (nonchalance).** The writer's labor doesn't show.
   Difficulty of research, agonies of drafting, throat-clearing about how hard the
   topic is — all hidden. The reader sees the finished view, not the scaffolding.
6. **The prose is about the world, not about itself.** No commentary on the
   writing ("In this essay I will argue…", "It is interesting to note…"). Show the
   thing.

### What this commits you to, concretely

- **Concrete over abstract.** Show things a reader can see, count, and touch.
  Default to the visual and the particular; reach for the abstraction only after
  the example. *"The cost fell by half"* beats *"a reduction in the cost metric
  was observed."*
- **The reader is intelligent and uninformed** — never talk down, never lecture,
  but never assume they already share your context (that's the curse of knowledge,
  §2).
- **Commit to claims.** Classic style states things. It doesn't cower behind
  *somewhat, arguably, in a sense, it could be said that.* (This is not a licence
  to misstate certainty — see the nuance below.)

### The styles to avoid (so you can recognize when you've slipped)

Pinker contrasts classic style with stances that smudge the window. The one that
ruins most real-world prose is the first:

- **Self-conscious / reflexive style** — prose about the prose. Hedging,
  apologizing, signposting, metadiscourse ("This paper is organized as follows"),
  and *professional narcissism* (the writer's field, methods, and anxieties
  on display instead of the subject). The reader is forced to watch the writer
  writing. **This is the default failure mode of academic and AI prose.**
- **Practical style** — pure utility (memos, forms): fine for its purpose, but
  flat and formulaic if used everywhere.
- **Plain style** — everything stated outright, no nuance, no trust in the reader
  to see; distrusts metaphor and subtlety.
- **Postmodern / oracular / prophetic styles** — prose that denies that truth is
  knowable, or that performs the writer's superior vision. Self-undermining and
  alienating.

### The honest caveats (classic style is a model, not a religion)

Pinker is explicit: classic style is a **useful pretense**, not a universal law.

- It suits **exposition and most narrative non-fiction** best. Poetry, some
  fiction, and genuinely **self-referential or deeply contested** material may
  legitimately need a different stance.
- "Truth is presentable" is an *operating assumption*, not a metaphysical claim.
  When your material really is uncertain, **say so accurately** — classic style
  forbids *compulsive, defensive* hedging, not *honest* qualification. The skill
  is to qualify once, precisely, and move on, rather than to coat every sentence
  in protective fuzz.

**Operational rules**
1. Before drafting, name your stance: "I am showing one intelligent reader X in
   the world." Hold it.
2. Delete every sentence that comments on the writing rather than the subject.
3. Lead with the concrete; introduce abstraction only after a picture.
4. State claims plainly; qualify at most once, precisely, where the truth requires
   it — never reflexively.
5. Scan for the self-conscious tells: metadiscourse, apologies, signposting,
   field-narcissism. Cut them.

## 2. The curse of knowledge — the chief cause of bad writing

> "The curse of knowledge [is] the single best explanation of why good people
> write bad prose. It is the difficulty of imagining what it is like for someone
> else not to know something that you know."

This is the most important idea in the book. **You cannot directly feel your
reader's ignorance**, because once you know something you can't un-know it to
simulate not knowing it. So good intentions ("I'll keep the reader in mind")
aren't enough — your mind is *exactly* the instrument that's compromised.

### Why expertise actively makes it worse

As you master something, you start thinking about it in terms of *what you use it
for* rather than *what it looks like and is made of*. You compress ideas into
mental "chunks" with private labels, and you forget that those labels are private.
The result: jargon, undefined abbreviations, skipped steps, and unexplained
"obvious" leaps. The reader, lacking your chunks, falls off.

### How it shows up (recognize these in your own drafts)

- **Undefined jargon and abbreviations** used on first mention.
- **Skipped steps** in reasoning ("obviously," "clearly," "it follows that") where
  the link is only obvious to you.
- **Functional fixedness:** naming things by their role in your project, not by
  what they are, so the reader can't picture them.
- **Burying the point:** describing details before telling the reader *what they're
  looking at and why* — no framing, so the details land as noise.
- **Abstraction-stacking:** referring to ideas with metaconcepts ("approach,"
  "framework," "factor," "issue") instead of the concrete thing.

### The cures (these are the real fix — Pinker is emphatic that introspection is not)

1. **Get a draft in front of real readers and watch where they stumble.** Their
   confusion is data your intuition can't supply. (For an AI: simulate a specific
   uninformed reader and re-read literally, flagging every term that reader
   couldn't define.)
2. **Become a stranger to your own text:** let it cool, then read it cold — ideally
   aloud. Time and the ear expose what familiarity hides.
3. **Define jargon and spell out abbreviations on first use** — or, better,
   replace them with plain words.
4. **Use concrete language and examples.** A picture or instance before the
   abstraction gives the reader something to hang the concept on.
5. **Name the point before the details.** Tell the reader what they're about to
   look at and why it matters, then show it.
6. **Unpack your chunks.** Where you've compressed an idea into a label, expand it
   at least once into what it actually is.

**Operational rules**
1. On every first mention of a term, ask: would a smart non-specialist know this?
   If not, define it or replace it.
2. Delete *obviously / clearly / of course / it follows that* unless the step
   really is shown.
3. Re-read as a specific uninformed reader; flag every undefined chunk and every
   detail that arrives before its frame.
4. Put a concrete example beside every abstraction that carries weight.
5. Always cool-and-reread (or read aloud) before declaring a draft clear.

## 3. The web, the tree, and the string — syntax that serves the reader

Pinker's model of a sentence: in your head ideas form a **web** (a tangle of
related concepts, unordered). To communicate it you must flatten it into a
**string** (one word after another, the only thing the reader actually receives).
Between them sits the **tree**: the hierarchical phrase structure of syntax that
groups words into nested constituents. **The reader's job is to reverse-engineer
your tree from your string.** Good syntax makes that reconstruction easy; bad
syntax overloads it.

### The core constraint: working memory

The reader holds only a few things in mind at once. Every time your sentence opens
a phrase that isn't yet completed, the reader must keep it "open" until it
resolves. Pile up open phrases and the reader runs out of memory and loses the
thread. So the master rule is: **minimize how much the reader must hold open at
any moment.**

### The concrete moves

- **Prefer right-branching; avoid center-embedding.** A *right-branching* sentence
  adds modifiers after their heads, so the reader resolves each piece as they go.
  *Center-embedding* nests a clause inside another, interrupting it, forcing the
  reader to hold the outer clause open across the inner one.
  - *Center-embedded (hard):* "The report the analyst the firm hired wrote was
    late."
  - *Right-branching (easy):* "The report was late — the one written by the
    analyst the firm had hired."
- **Avoid garden-path sentences.** These lead the reader into a parse that turns
  out wrong, forcing a costly re-read. *"The man who hunts ducks out on
  weekends."* (You parse "hunts ducks," then must back up.) Re-order or
  re-punctuate so the first parse is the right one.
- **Resolve ambiguity.** If a modifier could attach in two places, the reader
  picks one — sometimes the wrong one. *"Students who study rarely pass"* — rarely
  study, or rarely pass? Move the adverb or recast.
- **Light before heavy; save the heaviest constituent for the end.** Put short
  elements first and the long, complex one last, so no big structure is left
  hanging. This is why "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" works and
  "the pursuit of happiness, life, and liberty" thuds.
- **Match the grammatical subject to the discourse topic.** The thing the sentence
  is *about* should usually be its subject — even if that means using the passive
  (see below).
- **Beware noun piles and agreement-over-distance.** Long noun-noun-noun strings
  ("patient data access request form") force the reader to guess the structure.
  And when subject and verb are far apart, "tree-blindness" causes agreement
  errors ("the *set* of results *are*…" → *is*).

### Pinker's nuance on the passive voice (do not blanket-ban it)

The passive is **not** a defect. It is a tool for two legitimate jobs: (1) when the
doer is unknown, irrelevant, or obvious ("the experiment was repeated"), and (2)
to keep the sentence's topic in subject position and honor given-before-new (§4).
*"The bill was vetoed by the governor"* is right when the bill is the topic. Use
the active by default for energy and clarity; reach for the passive deliberately
when it manages topic and flow better.

**Operational rules**
1. Keep phrases right-branching; never nest a clause inside another if you can
   append it instead.
2. Read each sentence for a wrong first parse (garden path) and for two-way
   modifier attachment (ambiguity); recast if found.
3. Put the longest, heaviest element last.
4. Make the subject the thing the sentence is about; use the passive when that
   requires it, not as a habit.
5. Break noun piles into prepositional phrases; check subject-verb agreement
   across any long gap.

## 4. Arcs of coherence — making sentences add up to a text

A sequence of individually clear sentences can still be incoherent. Coherence is
what lets the reader assemble the sentences into **one mental model**. Pinker's
machinery:

### Topic and point

A passage needs a **consistent topic** (what it's about) and a **point** (what
you're saying about it). The reader should always know both. Drifting topic or a
missing point is the commonest cause of "well-written but I don't know what
they're getting at."

### The given–new contract

Begin sentences with **given** information (what the reader already knows from
prior text) and end with **new** information. This threads sentences together: the
new element of one becomes the given element that opens the next. Violating it —
opening with the brand-new thing — makes prose feel disjointed even when each
sentence is fine. (This is another reason the passive sometimes wins: it lets you
front the given topic.)

- *Disjointed:* "A virus causes the disease. Mosquitoes carry the virus.
  Stagnant water breeds mosquitoes."
- *Threaded:* "The disease is caused by a virus. The virus is carried by
  mosquitoes, which breed in stagnant water." (each new item becomes the next
  given)

### Coherence connectives are traffic signals — don't omit them

The logical relation between two sentences (cause, contrast, elaboration,
example, concession, sequence) must be **marked**, not left for the reader to
guess. *because, so, therefore, but, however, nevertheless, for example, in other
words, first… then…* are not clutter; they are the signage that tells the reader
how each new sentence relates to the last. Cutting them in the name of concision
produces prose that is terse and incoherent. Common relations to signal:
similarity, contrast, elaboration, exemplification, generalization, cause,
result, **violated expectation** (concession), and sequence.

### Consistency of reference — no "elegant variation"

Refer to the same thing by the **same name** every time. The old advice to vary
your terms ("the cello… the instrument… the wooden creation…") backfires: each new
label makes the reader wonder whether a *new* entity has entered. In expository
prose, repetition of the key term is a feature, not a flaw.

### The right amount of detail, and stating things positively

- **Calibrate detail to the reader's model:** too much buries the point; too little
  leaves gaps the reader can't bridge. Give what's needed to build the model, no
  more.
- **Prefer positive statements.** Negation is harder to process; "*remembered to
  save*" is easier than "*did not forget to save*." State what *is* rather than
  what *isn't* where you can.

### Reveal structure through the prose, not through metadiscourse

Classic style shows organization by the *order and connection* of its content, not
by announcing it ("In this section, I will first… second…"). Let the arc be felt.
Use the occasional genuine signpost only when the structure is too complex to feel
otherwise — and then sparingly.

**Operational rules**
1. State the topic and the point of every paragraph; if you can't, the paragraph
   isn't ready.
2. Order information given-before-new; let each sentence's new element seed the
   next.
3. Mark the logical relation between consecutive sentences with an explicit
   connective; never make the reader infer cause/contrast/concession.
4. Name each recurring thing with one consistent term; kill elegant variation.
5. Prefer positive phrasing; cut needless negation.
6. Reveal structure through content order, not through "this paper will…"
   signposting.

## 5. Telling right from wrong — a sane theory of usage

Pinker's position dissolves the fake war between "anything goes" and "language is
decaying." Caring about correct usage is right; obeying bogus rules is not. **Judge
every supposed rule by whether it serves the reader** — clarity, grace, and
consistency with the practice of careful writers — not by whether some 18th-century
authority decreed it.

### The test for any rule

Ask: (1) Do careful writers and speakers actually observe it? (2) Does following it
make the writing clearer or more graceful? (3) Or is it a superstition — imported
from Latin, invented to seem learned, or a confusion of formal with correct? Keep
the first; ignore the third.

### Myths you may ignore (Pinker debunks these)

- **Splitting infinitives** — "to boldly go" is fine; the ban is a Latin hangover.
- **Ending a sentence with a preposition** — "the thing I was looking *for*" is
  correct; the ban is a superstition.
- **Beginning a sentence with a conjunction** — *And, But, Because* at the start
  are legitimate and long-used.
- **"Between you and me"** is correct (object case); "between you and I" is a
  hypercorrection. (Conversely, don't use "I" in object position to sound proper.)
- **Singular "they"** — has centuries of good-writer pedigree; use it.
- **"Hopefully"** as a sentence adverb ("Hopefully, it will rain") is fine.
- **"That" vs "which"** — the restrictive-*that* / nonrestrictive-*which* split is
  a useful American copyediting convention, not a law; the *real* rule is to set
  off nonrestrictive clauses with commas. Don't agonize over restrictive "which."
- **"Who/whom"** — "whom" is fading; use it where it's clearly the object in formal
  writing, but "who" is increasingly acceptable.
- **"Less/fewer"** — "fewer" for count nouns is a tidy convention worth following
  in careful prose, but "less" with counts is old and not a sin ("10 items or
  less").

### Rules worth keeping (because they serve clarity or credibility)

- **Subject–verb and pronoun agreement.**
- **Pronoun case** where it's unambiguous (don't write "Me and him went").
- **Parallel structure** in coordinated lists ("to swim, to bike, and to run," not
  "to swim, biking, and a run").
- **Dangling modifiers when they genuinely mislead** ("Turning the corner, the
  clock tower came into view" — the tower didn't turn). A dangler that creates no
  confusion is venial; one that creates a wrong picture is a real defect.
- **The small correctness signals** — *its/it's, their/there/they're,* apostrophes,
  homophones. Pinker's pragmatic point: such errors don't muddle meaning much, but
  they make careful readers distrust you, so fix them.

**The governing principle:** usage exists to serve the reader. Follow the rules
that aid comprehension or preserve your credibility; drop the ones that are mere
shibboleths; and above all, be **consistent** and guided by **evidence of how
careful writers actually write**.

**Operational rules**
1. For any "rule" you're about to apply, run the three-part test; if it's a
   superstition, ignore it.
2. Never hypercorrect ("between you and I," "whom" where "who" is the subject).
3. Keep agreement, case, and parallelism; fix danglers that mislead.
4. Eliminate the small homophone/apostrophe errors — they cost credibility.
5. Be consistent within a document.

## 6. Pinker's catalogue of prose sins — a consolidated hit-list

These recur across the book. Treat each as a defect with a fix — and note the
nuance, because Pinker rejects mechanical bans.

- **Metadiscourse & signposting** ("This essay will…", "As noted above…",
  "It is important to note that…"). → Cut; reveal structure through content.
- **Compulsive hedging** ("somewhat," "rather," "arguably," "in a sense," "it
  could be argued"). → State the claim, or make a smaller claim you can stand
  behind. *Nuance:* keep one honest qualifier where the truth is genuinely
  uncertain.
- **Apologizing** ("I am no expert, but…", "this is only a rough sketch"). → Delete;
  earn trust by showing, not disclaiming.
- **Professional narcissism** (your field's debates, methods, and anxieties instead
  of the subject). → Spend the reader's attention on the world.
- **Zombie nouns (nominalizations)** — verbs turned into abstract nouns
  ("the *cancellation* of the meeting was effected" → "the meeting was
  cancelled"). → Turn them back into verbs. *Nuance:* a nominalization is fine when
  the concept genuinely is the thing under discussion ("the *cancellation* angered
  investors").
- **Passive overuse** — see §3. → Default active; keep the passive where it manages
  topic/given-new or hides an irrelevant doer. Not a blanket sin.
- **Abstractitis / metaconcepts** ("approach, framework, level, factor, issue,
  process, activity") padding sentences. → Replace with the concrete thing.
- **Clichés and mixed metaphors.** → Fresh, consistent imagery, or none. A dead
  metaphor noticed mid-sentence ("ironing out bottlenecks") jolts the reader.
- **Inflated diction** ("utilize, facilitate, leverage, commence, in order to"). →
  "use, help, …, begin, to."
- **Needless negation.** → State positively.
- **Elegant variation.** → One name per thing.
- **The curse of knowledge** in all its forms (§2) — the master sin behind most of
  the others.

## 7. The revision protocol

Run these passes in order. Each maps to a section above.

1. **Stance (§1).** Is this a window on the world, or is it about itself? Cut
   metadiscourse, apologies, hedging, field-narcissism. Are claims committed?
2. **Curse of knowledge (§2).** Re-read as a specific smart-but-uninformed reader.
   Define or replace every undefined term; frame details before delivering them;
   put an example beside every load-bearing abstraction.
3. **Concreteness.** Replace metaconcepts and zombie nouns with things and verbs
   (except where the abstraction is genuinely the subject).
4. **Syntax (§3).** Right-branch; kill garden paths and ambiguity; heaviest
   element last; subject = topic; check agreement across distance.
5. **Coherence (§4).** Topic and point clear per paragraph; given-before-new;
   explicit connectives between sentences; one name per thing; positive phrasing.
6. **Usage (§5).** Fix real errors (agreement, case, parallelism, misleading
   danglers, homophones); ignore the myths; don't hypercorrect; be consistent.
7. **The ear.** Read it aloud (or simulate it). Fix the first place the voice
   stumbles, drones, or runs out of breath. Repeat until it reads clean.

**The single test that subsumes the rest** (Pinker's own): *read your prose as if
it were a window onto the world.* If a sentence makes you think about the writing —
its caution, its cleverness, its scaffolding — rather than the thing it shows, it
has failed. Rewrite until the glass is clear.
