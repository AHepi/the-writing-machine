## The record — theatre and conduct (serves D1, D3)

- **F1 [strong]:** In August 2017 Pauline Hanson sat in the Senate chamber wearing
  a full burqa for about ten minutes, then removed it to call for the garment to
  be banned. The Coalition's own Attorney-General, George Brandis, condemned the
  stunt as "appalling" — "to mock its religious garments is an appalling thing to
  do" — and drew a standing ovation from across the chamber. [src: Fortune2017, BBC2017]

- **F2 [medium]:** The burqa stunt was rebuked not by the left alone but by her own
  side of politics: Brandis was the sitting Liberal Attorney-General. [src: Fortune2017, BBC2017]

## The record — foreign money and Australia's gun laws (serves D1, D3, D4)

- **F3 [strong]:** Al Jazeera's 2019 investigation *How to Sell a Massacre*
  secretly filmed One Nation's then–chief of staff James Ashby and its Queensland
  leader Steve Dickson in Washington in September 2018 seeking up to **US$20
  million** from the US gun lobby, including the NRA. Ashby is recorded settling on
  "20" million; the discussed aim was funding to win the balance of power and soften
  Australia's gun laws. [src: AlJazeera2019, BBC2019]

- **F4 [strong]:** Australia's strict national firearms regime (the National
  Firearms Agreement) was created after the 1996 Port Arthur massacre under
  **conservative** Prime Minister John Howard — i.e. the laws One Nation figures
  discussed weakening for foreign cash are a Coalition achievement. [src: AJPortArthur2019]

- **F5 [strong]:** After the footage aired (March 2019), Hanson did not disown the
  approach — she called the investigation a "political attack" and said the
  comments were "taken out of context"; Ashby and Dickson said they had been
  filmed after drinking (the "alcohol" defence). [src: FoxAlcohol2019, AlJazeera2019]
  *Register note (C1): the money was solicited by her senior officials, not by
  Hanson personally; the draft must attribute the solicitation to Ashby/Dickson and
  attribute to Hanson her defence of them, not the ask itself.*

## The record — a party that can't hold together (serves D1, D3)

- **F6 [strong]:** In October 2017 the High Court disqualified One Nation senator
  Malcolm Roberts under s44 of the Constitution for holding **British citizenship**
  at his election — an eligibility/sovereignty failure inside a party built on
  sovereignty. [src: WikiRoberts, Conversation_Roberts]

- **F7 [medium]:** One Nation has fractured repeatedly. Rod Culleton left and was
  then disqualified (2016–17); Fraser Anning quit the party effectively the day he
  replaced Roberts (November 2017); Brian Burston quit in June 2018 after clashing
  with Hanson. [src: WikiONP, Conversation_Roberts]

- **F8 [medium]:** Burston's split centred on the Turnbull government's company tax
  cuts — a policy on which **Hanson had reversed her position** — a documented
  flip-flop on a major economic vote. [src: WikiONP]

## The record — importing the culture war (serves D1, D3, D4)

- **F9 [strong]:** In October 2018 Hanson moved a Senate motion declaring "it's OK
  to be white." 23 Coalition senators (including senior ministers) voted **for** it
  before the government reversed the next day, calling the votes an administrative
  "error" that "slipped through." The motion was narrowly defeated 31–28. [src: BBC2018, Buzzfeed2018]

- **F10 [medium]:** Reporting and academic analysis trace the "it's OK to be white"
  slogan to online white-supremacist forums, where it was designed to provoke. The
  episode dragged the mainstream centre-right into an embarrassing backflip.
  [src: BBC2018, Macquarie2021]
  *Register note (C2): the slogan-origin is reported/academic, and Hanson framed
  the motion as anti-racism; attribute the origin to reporting, not to her stated
  intent.*

## Tenure and what she's actually delivered (serves D2, D4 — the honest caveat)

- **F11 [strong]:** Hanson is a long-running national figure — in the House of
  Representatives 1996–1998 and a Senator for Queensland continuously since July
  2016 — with patriotism and immigration as her core brand. [src: Britannica, WikiONP]

- **F12 [weak — self-reported]:** One Nation's concrete legislative claims are
  modest and leverage-based — e.g. it takes credit for a four-year wait before new
  migrants can claim welfare and for various crossbench deals. The "achievements"
  list is published by the party itself. [src: OneNation_highlights]
  *Stake note: source is the party; do not state these as independently verified
  wins. Use only to concede she has had some influence.*

- **F13 [medium]:** Hanson's appeal rests on real, widely-felt concerns the major
  parties were slow to engage — the pace of immigration, social cohesion, and a
  sense that the political class had stopped listening to ordinary people. This is
  the strongest version of the "she's pro-Australia" case and must be granted
  before it is answered. [src: Conversation2020, Britannica]
  *Stake note: Conversation2020 is analysis with a critical POV; used here only for
  the uncontested point that these concerns drive her support.*
