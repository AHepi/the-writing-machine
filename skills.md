# Writing Craft — an operating manual for an AI writer

This skill turns seven domains of writing craft into rules you can *apply* and
*check*, not just admire. Each module is grounded in the named authorities at its
end. The guiding stance throughout: **the reader is a competent equal you are
showing something to**, and craft is mostly the disciplined removal of whatever
breaks that view.

---

## 0. How to use this skill

**Apply macro → micro.** When drafting or revising, work in this order; later
levels assume earlier ones are settled:

1. **Narrative Architecture** — is the structure causal and shaped?
2. **Perspective & Psychic Distance** — whose eyes, how close, held steadily?
3. **Exposition & Subtext** — what is told vs. withheld vs. implied?
4. **Prose** — sentence-by-sentence strength.
5. **Cadence & Prosody** — the rhythm and sound of the sentences.
6. **Punctuation Infrastructure** — the breath, pace, and white space that carry it.

**Two master diagnostics that subsume most others:**

- **Read it aloud (simulate the mind's ear).** Almost every authority converges
  here. Where you stumble, a sentence is mis-built; where you drone, lengths are
  uniform; where you run out of breath, a mark is missing.
- **The deletion test.** Remove the word, sentence, fact, image, or scene. If
  nothing downstream breaks and nothing is lost, it was decoration. Cut it.

### Universal AI / amateur tells — scan every draft for these

These are the contemporary fingerprints of generated and weak prose. Treat each
as a defect to be removed, not a style:

- **Hedging & inflation:** *it's worth noting, it's important to, plays a crucial/
  vital role, in the realm of, one might argue,* plus inflated verbs *delve,
  leverage, utilize, facilitate,* and reflexive connectives *moreover, furthermore,
  additionally.* Cut or replace.
- **Abstraction over the concrete:** category-words and nominalizations where a
  named, sensory thing belongs.
- **Em-dash flood:** LLMs use em dashes at 3–5× the human rate, often to *restate*
  rather than add. Cap at ~1 per paragraph; every dash must add new information.
- **Filtering verbs:** *she saw / felt / heard / noticed / realized / wondered* —
  a camera inserted between reader and experience.
- **Over-modification:** adjective stacks and *-ly* adverbs patching weak verbs.
- **Monotony:** four-plus sentences of the same length in a row; every sentence
  ending on a period; uniform paragraph size.
- **On-the-nose statement:** characters (or narration) naming feelings, wants, and
  themes outright, leaving the reader nothing to infer.
- **Uniform caution & smoothness:** no risk, no texture, no silence — prose that
  is correct and dead.

## 1. Prose

**What it is.** Craft at the level of the word and the sentence — the actual
texture the reader meets.

**Why it matters.** "Good writing is just one strong sentence after another"
(Klinkenborg). The sentence is the unit of composition; everything larger is
built from it.

**Principles**

- **Concrete beats abstract, always.** Specific sensory detail sustains Gardner's
  "vivid and continuous dream"; abstraction breaks it. Name the thing.
- **Verbs and nouns carry meaning; modifiers leak it.** One strong verb beats a
  weak verb plus an adverb. "Walked slowly and quietly" → "crept."
- **Every word earns its place.** Concision is the removal of what isn't working,
  not brevity for its own sake.
- **Beat the curse of knowledge** (Pinker): you cannot feel what the reader
  doesn't know. Give a concrete example before the abstraction.
- **Default to classic style** (Pinker): prose as a clean window onto the world,
  writer and reader as equals — not *plain* style (flat, nuance-free) and not
  *self-conscious* style (prose about its own difficulty: "In this essay I will…").

**Advanced techniques**

- **The end of the sentence is the power position; the start is second** (Clark's
  2-3-1 order): strongest material last, second-strongest first, weakest buried in
  the middle. The period spotlights whatever sits beside it.
- **Imply, don't state.** "The longer the sentence, the less it's able to imply"
  (Klinkenborg). Short sentences leave silence the reader fills.
- **Make syntax enact meaning** (Le Guin / Fish): clipped clauses for jerky
  action; a long, accumulating sentence for flow. Choose *subordinating* syntax
  (logical, hierarchical) for argument, *additive* syntax (clauses rambling on)
  for mood.
- **Telling, done right.** "Show, don't tell" is a technique, not a law. Summary
  *telling* correctly handles transitions, time-compression, and low-stakes
  connective tissue; reserve dramatized *scene* for the moments that matter.
  Interiority is often mislabeled "telling" when it is the deepest showing.

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *Filtering:* "She saw the door was ajar and felt a chill." → "The door stood
  ajar. A chill ran down her spine." (Keep the filter only when *perceiving* is
  the action: "She strained to hear the footsteps stop.")
- *Over-modification:* "He ran very quickly across the large, open field." → "He
  sprinted across the field."
- *Abstraction/hedging:* "It's important to note that the data plays a crucial
  role in informing decisions." → "The data drives the decision."

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. Cut any word removable without loss. Prefer the shorter true sentence.
2. Replace weak-verb-plus-adverb with one strong verb; flag every *-ly* adverb.
3. Delete filtering verbs unless perception is the point.
4. Replace each abstraction with a concrete noun or named instance.
5. Ban the hedge/inflation set (see §0) except where genuinely needed.
6. Put the most important word last, the second-most first.
7. Choose telling for transitions/low stakes; scene for load-bearing moments.

**Self-checks** — Which words can I cut and lose nothing? Does each sentence carry
one strong verb? Where am I naming an emotion instead of rendering its cause? Is
the strongest word at the end? Is this a window on the world or throat-clearing?

**Authorities** — Klinkenborg (*Several Short Sentences*): one strong sentence
after another. Pinker (*The Sense of Style*): classic style; beat the curse of
knowledge. Gardner (*The Art of Fiction*): sustain the vivid, continuous dream.
Le Guin (*Steering the Craft*): sound and syntax enact the action. Fish (*How to
Write a Sentence*): subordinating vs. additive — form is meaning. Clark (*Writing
Tools*): activate verbs; strong words first and last. Saunders: vagueness is the
enemy. Hale (*Sin and Syntax*): lean on verbs.

## 2. Cadence & Prosody

**What it is.** The rhythm, pace, and sound of prose — its music.

**Why it matters.** Skilled readers subvocalize; the "mind's ear" registers
rhythm even silently. Sound is not laid over meaning — it *is* meaning (Frost's
"sound of sense": cadence carries sense independent of the words).

**Principles**

- **Rhythm lives mostly in sentence-length variation and pause placement,** with
  stress patterning a secondary, sentence-level effect. Do not over-index on
  syllable scansion.
- **Variation is the engine.** Uniform length — short *or* long — is the cardinal
  fault. The pleasure is in the *change*.
- **End on strength.** The full stop magnifies the last word; the effect peaks at
  a paragraph's end, against the white space. End on a stressed content word, not
  a preposition or qualifier.
- **Cadence must match content.** Action/tension → short, clipped, end-stressed.
  Reflection/grandeur → long, cumulative, comma-braided.

**Advanced techniques**

- **Cumulative vs. periodic sentences.** A *cumulative* sentence states its core
  then trails free modifiers (spoken, breathing outward); a *periodic* sentence
  withholds completion to the end (suspense, delayed punch).
- **Tricolon and the rhythm of three** — the smallest complete pattern. Make
  members ascend in length to build momentum; end short to land hard.
- **Parallelism / anaphora** create an audible downbeat — powerful but
  self-announcing; reserve for genuine climaxes.
- **The deliberate short sentence** after a long stretch: a rest, then a hit.
- **Sound texture at homeopathic doses:** assonance, consonance, alliteration to
  slow the ear where you want dwell-time — one motif per sentence, ≤3 stresses.

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *Accidental meter* (prose slipping into sing-song iambics): perturb it — swap a
  word, add or cut a syllable.
- *Monotonous length:* if three consecutive sentences are within ~3 words of each
  other, rewrite one.
- *Weak endings:* "...important to him, in a lot of ways, actually." → "To him it
  mattered more than anything." (lands on *anything*).
- *All-short thump:* "She walked into the room and she saw the body and she
  screamed and she ran." → "She walked in. She saw the body. She screamed and
  ran." (ends on *ran*).

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. Vary sentence length within every paragraph; no three consecutive sentences
   within ±3 words. Include at least one short (<8 words) and one long (>25) per
   ~150 words.
2. After any sentence ≥30 words, place a short one (≤8 words).
3. End sentences on a stressed content word; end paragraphs on the strongest word.
4. Use tricolon (not pairs/quads) for lists meant to feel complete; ascend in
   length for build.
5. Cap audible sound devices at one motif per sentence; ≤3 alliterative stresses.
6. If ≥10 consecutive syllables alternate stress regularly, break the meter.
7. Match pause-weight to tempo: comma = short rest, semicolon/dash = medium,
   period = full rest.

**Self-checks** — Read aloud: where does it drone, stumble, or fall flat?
Length-map the paragraph (write each sentence's word count): flat line = fix.
Last-word audit: list every final word; flag weak tails. "Voices behind a door"
(Frost): would the intonation alone still carry the intended sense?

**Authorities** — Le Guin: the sound of the language is where it begins; vary
length. Frost: the sound of sense. Klinkenborg: attend to rhythm first and last.
V. Tufte (*Artful Sentences*): syntax is style — cumulative/periodic/parallel are
instruments. Clark: order words for emphasis (2-3-1). M. Oliver (*A Poetry
Handbook*): borrow stress and sound-weight from verse. Hale: make grammar sing.

## 3. Subtext

**What it is.** The unsaid beneath the said — the desires, fears, and meanings
the surface of dialogue and action only points at.

**Why it matters.** McKee's law: *"If the scene is about what the scene is about,
you're in deep shit."* On-the-nose writing collapses text and subtext into one
layer and leaves the reader nothing to do. Engagement comes from the reader
knowing more than the characters admit.

**Principles**

- **Text vs. subtext.** Text is the said and the surface behavior; subtext is the
  unsaid. A good scene is a *transparency* — the reader looks *through* the words
  to the real life underneath.
- **Desire drives, behavior conceals.** Characters almost never name their want
  (Stanislavski: ~10% of intent is spoken). "You look well!" played as *"I need to
  butter you up before I ask for money."*
- **Iceberg / theory of omission** (Hemingway). Know everything; write a fraction;
  the reader *feels* the submerged mass. Crucial guardrail: **omission** (cutting
  what you generated) creates presence; **absence** (never knowing it) creates
  thinness. You may only leave out what you actually know.

**Advanced techniques (how to build subtext mechanically)**

- **Two wants in conflict + a displacement topic.** Give each character an
  unvoiceable goal, then stage the scene over something trivial (coffee, a chore).
  The buried wants press on the surface and load every line.
- **Write it on the nose, then bury it.** Draft the naked statement, delete it,
  and rewrite so the want shows only through *tactic*. Keep the version with the
  widest gap between said and meant.
- **Conflict-load exposition:** never let characters agree about a fact; have them
  react differently so information arrives as friction.
- **Staging** (Baxter): bodies in space — distance, what they touch, where they
  look, the pause before the line — say what characters can't.
- **Objects/gesture/setting as carriers:** the untouched ring, the table kept
  between them. Particular detail implies an interior (Stein).
- **In non-dialogue prose,** route feeling through *selection* of perception: a
  grieving POV notices the wrong small things. Don't name the emotion.

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *Naming the feeling:* "I'm angry you lied to me." → "I made two plates. I'll
  wrap yours." (anger displaced into a domestic act).
- *Exposition-as-confession:* "As you know, since Dad died, we've been broke." →
  "You're still parking in the garage?" / "It's covered." / "Is it."
- *Therapy-speak* (characters diagnosing their own psychology): replace insight
  with maneuver — real people defend, deflect, joke.
- *Confusion masquerading as mystery:* withholding is fair only if the answer is
  seeded and recoverable on reread.

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. For any charged scene, write each character's unspoken want in one line; never
   let them say it.
2. Choose a surface topic ≠ the real subject. If equal, change the surface.
3. Convert every emotion-naming line into a tactic or action.
4. Make shared information arrive through disagreement, never mutual recap.
5. Add one staging/object beat per scene that undercuts the dialogue.
6. Before omitting, write the submerged fact down somewhere; only omit what you
   know. Plant a recoverable clue for anything withheld.
7. **Switch to text at the rupture.** At climax/confession, let the buried want
   surface plainly — earned directness lands *because* subtext built the pressure.
   Do not run subtext everywhere.

**Self-checks** — Can I delete every line where a character names a feeling and
lose nothing? Is the scene "about what it's about"? Does the reader know something
a character hides from themselves? Under each spoken line, is there a different
sentence? Is anything withheld findable on reread? Did I write the iceberg before
cutting it?

**Authorities** — McKee (*Dialogue*, *Story*): a scene is never about what it's
literally about. Hemingway: know everything, show the tip. Baxter (*The Art of
Subtext*): staging speaks the unspoken. Stanislavski: every line played for its
objective. Seger (*Writing Subtext*): gesture, image, loaded word choice. Stein:
particularity implies interiority. Fair-play tradition: seed clues, or it's a
cheat.

## 4. Exposition

**What it is.** The delivery of information — backstory, world, mechanics —
without clogging momentum.

**Why it matters.** The reader tolerates information only in exchange for
momentum. The brain treats every detail as a question — "why am I being told
this?" — and retains it **only when it has stakes** (Cron). No stakes, no
retention.

**Principles**

- **Information is interesting only when wanted.** Create the *question* first;
  then the fact arrives as relief, not homework. **Carry a stone, not a wall** —
  deliver the shard needed *now*, never the quarry.
- **Generation is downstream of need** (Le Guin): first decide whether the info is
  necessary at all. Most infodumps are the writer's research pride.
- **Show the consequence, imply the cause.** A concrete detail in motion ("she
  flinched when the gate buzzed") replaces a paragraph of backstory.

**Advanced techniques**

- **Incluing** (Walton, from Damon Knight): scatter information in fragments so
  the picture *accretes* in the reader's mind. The SF/F reading protocol — show
  the world sideways, never lecture.
- **Exposition as ammunition** (McKee): characters wield what they know as
  *weapons* in pursuit of desire; the fact emerges at a moment of conflict. "I am
  your father" lands because it is deployed to win a fight, not to inform.
- **Just-in-time delivery + strategic withholding.** Release each fact at the last
  responsible moment. An unexplained gap is a promise that pulls the reader on.
- **Dose by thread type** (Sanderson's MICE): a *Milieu* story earns more setting
  detail; an *Event/Inquiry* story withholds and meters.
- **Dramatize the rule's violation** and let the reader infer the rule from the
  reaction, rather than stating it.

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *"As you know, Bob" / maid-and-butler:* characters tell each other what both
  know. → Give one party genuine ignorance, or inject conflict:
  "You signed off on that reactor." / "The accident was before my contract." /
  "Tell that to the families."
- *Opening infodump:* history before the reader cares. → Start in scene; backfill
  only when a stake demands it.
- *Infodump → inclued:* "The Empire had banned magic three centuries ago after the
  Sundering; offenders were branded and exiled." → "She pulled her sleeve down over
  the brand before the guard turned. Three hundred years, and they still burned you
  for it."
- *Stakeless fact → staked:* "The bridge was built in 1840, the only crossing for
  forty miles." → "Forty miles of river, one bridge, and it was already on fire
  behind them."

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. Pose a question in the reader before delivering the fact that answers it.
2. Deliver only what the current beat requires; defer the rest.
3. Every exposed fact must change what a character wants, fears, or can do — or
   cut it.
4. Prefer revealing information through a character *using* it in conflict.
5. Never have characters state what the listener already knows.
6. No unbroken expository passage beyond ~3 sentences without action, dialogue-
   conflict, or a sensory anchor.
7. Summarize the inert; dramatize the pivotal. Insert backstory/flashback only
   after a present-scene question makes the reader want it.

**Self-checks** — "Why now?": did the reader *just* ask this? Deletion test: does
a later beat break without this fact? "As you know" sniff: does anyone state
shared knowledge? Stakes audit: can I name what this fact changes for the
protagonist? Wall-vs-stone: is the longest info passage over ~3 sentences?

**Authorities** — Walton/Knight: incluing — let the picture accrete. McKee:
exposition is ammunition. Cron (*Wired for Story*): only staked information is
retained. Le Guin: ask if it's necessary; give it by hint. Sanderson (MICE):
meter info against thread type. "As you know, Bob": relocate shared knowledge to
conflict or narration.

## 5. Narrative Architecture

**What it is.** Story structure at every scale — beat, scene, sequence, act,
whole.

**Why it matters.** Structure is a managed sequence of *change*. Get the
architecture wrong and no sentence-level polish saves it.

**Principles (model-agnostic, load-bearing)**

- **Causality is the engine.** Scenes link by *therefore* or *but*, never *and
  then* (the Parker/Stone test). If "and then" fits between two beats, you have a
  list, not a plot.
- **The scene is a value-shift, not an event** (McKee). A scene that ends on the
  same charge it began with is non-functional — engineer the beat that flips a
  value (safe→endangered, hope→despair).
- **The shape is fractal** (Yorke). The same change-arc — want vs. obstacle vs.
  turn — recurs at beat, scene, act, and whole. This is the unifying insight that
  lets one set of checks work at every scale.
- **External plot exists to force internal change.** The protagonist *wants*
  something external and *needs* an internal correction (a misbelief to shed). The
  plot makes the want impossible until the need is faced. The live wire (Cron's
  "third rail") is the join between inner struggle and outer events.
- **Structure is a contract of promises.** Every setup is a debt; every payoff
  settles it (Chekhov's gun). Plant what you fire; fire what you plant.
- **Tension > surprise** (Hitchcock): suspense (the reader knows the bomb is
  there) lasts a whole scene; surprise lasts ten seconds — but a promised bomb
  must go off.

**Advanced techniques**

- **Two interlocking scene grammars** (Swain/Bickham): *Scene* = Goal → Conflict →
  Disaster (proactive); *Sequel* = Reaction → Dilemma → Decision (reactive
  transition that converts a disaster into the next goal). Alternating them gives
  prose its breathe-in/breathe-out rhythm.
- **Escalating try/fail cycles:** each attempt resolves "Yes, but…" (won, new
  cost) or "No, and…" (lost, worse) — never a flat yes/no — with stakes strictly
  rising. Convention: at least three before the climax.
- **Midpoint reversal:** a false victory or false defeat that flips the
  protagonist from reactive to proactive and mirrors the ending inversely.
- **All-is-lost before resolution:** the lowest point, where the want feels dead,
  is exactly where the need becomes available. Place the internal turn here.
- **Conflict-optional structure — kishōtenketsu:** Ki (setup) → Shō (development)
  → **Ten (twist/recontextualization)** → Ketsu (reconciliation). Tension comes
  from *juxtaposition*, not antagonism — ideal for slice-of-life, contemplative,
  comedic, or mystery-of-understanding pieces where forced conflict would falsify
  the tone.

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *"And then" sequencing* → rewrite each transition as *but/therefore*; cut any
  scene that survives the deletion test.
- *Flat scenes (no turn)* → assign each scene an opening value and an opposite
  closing value; build the flipping beat.
- *Repetitive try/fails* → raise stakes, narrow options, or change the *kind* of
  failure each cycle.
- *Plot without need* (the template-hero trap, "narratively competent but
  psychologically shallow") → define the misbelief first; make the climax test the
  *need*, not just deliver the *want*.
- *Forcing conflict onto a contemplative piece* → switch to kishōtenketsu; keep
  tension in the juxtaposition; let *Ten* recontextualize rather than "solve."

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. Between adjacent scenes, write the connective; if it's "and then," restructure
   to "but"/"therefore."
2. For each scene, record `value_in` and `value_out`; reject if equal.
3. Give every scene a goal, an obstacle, and a turn; add a sequel wherever the
   next goal isn't self-evident.
4. Build attempts as a ladder: each "yes-but"/"no-and," stakes greater than the
   last.
5. State the protagonist's *want* and *need/misbelief* in one line each before
   outlining; ensure the climax forces a choice between them.
6. Place a midpoint reversal and an all-is-lost low before the climax.
7. Log every setup with its payoff scene; flag orphans on both sides.
8. If the piece is contemplative, switch to kishōtenketsu and verify a real *Ten*
   exists with no antagonist smuggled in.

**Self-checks** — Can I delete this scene and lose nothing? What value flips here,
and on which beat? Is the link a therefore/but or a smuggled and-then? Is this
attempt costlier than the last? Does the climax test the *need*? Which promise
does this payoff settle; which plant is unfired? Suspense or mere surprise?

**Compact model comparison**

| Model | Core shape | Best when |
|---|---|---|
| **Three-act + Save the Cat** | Setup → confrontation (midpoint flip) → resolution; beat-driven | Commercial, plot-forward, screen-shaped work; risk = formulaic |
| **Story Circle / Hero's Journey** | Descent-and-return; protagonist crosses into the unknown, brings change back | Single-protagonist transformation, genre/adventure; risk = monomyth sameness, weak for ensemble/non-Western |
| **Kishōtenketsu** | Setup → development → recontextualizing twist → reconciliation; conflict-optional | Literary, slice-of-life, comedic, contemplative; where forced antagonism falsifies tone |

**Synthesis:** every model is a managed sequence of change in which units connect
by causality, each unit shifts a value, attempts escalate, setups pay off, and the
same shape nests from beat to whole. Models differ mainly in *what supplies the
change* (conflict vs. juxtaposition) and *whose change is centered* (single hero
vs. web of relations). Pick by tone; apply the invariants always.

**Authorities** — Parker/Stone: but/therefore, not and-then. McKee (*Story*): every
scene turns on a value. Yorke (*Into the Woods*): story is fractal. Harmon (Story
Circle): the 8-step want→change loop. Snyder (*Save the Cat!*): a map, not a cage.
Swain/Bickham: scene & sequel. Cron (*Story Genius*): the misbelief wired to the
plot. Kishōtenketsu: structure from juxtaposition. Le Guin (Carrier Bag Theory):
not every story is a spear — some are bags, tension from the relation among things.
Hitchcock/Chekhov: suspense beats surprise; every gun fires.

## 6. Perspective & Psychic Distance

**What it is.** Point of view and the felt gap between reader and a character's
interior.

**Why it matters.** Most POV errors come from conflating **who sees** (the
focalizer) with **who speaks** (the narrator) — Genette. Distance, once you can
name it, becomes a dial you set on purpose.

**Principles**

- **Separate focalizer from narrator.** A third-person narrator can speak while a
  character sees.
- **Psychic distance is a continuous dial, not a setting** (Gardner). You are
  always *at* some distance; the craft is choosing and changing it deliberately.
- **POV is a contract.** Whatever access you establish early (one head? all heads?
  camera only?), keep it; violations read as errors, not effects.
- **Interiority is the currency; voice carries distance.** Abstract/formal/Latinate
  diction = far; sensory/colloquial/fragmentary = near.

**Gardner's psychic-distance scale, made operational** (the canonical snowstorm
ladder). Level = f(naming + grammar of interiority + diction):

- **L1 — Remote / cinematic.** *"It was winter of the year 1853. A large man
  stepped out of a doorway."* Pure exterior. For openings, establishing shots,
  time-jumps, summary.
- **L2 — Identified, external.** *"Henry J. Warburton had never much cared for
  snowstorms."* Full name, reported disposition. To introduce and orient.
- **L3 — Limited interior, neutral.** *"Henry hated snowstorms."* First name, a
  stated feeling in plain narratorial grammar. The default working distance.
- **L4 — Free indirect, character's idiom.** *"God how he hated these damn
  snowstorms."* The narrator's grammar now carries the character's voice. For
  emotional engagement.
- **L5 — Deep immersion.** *"Snow. Down inside your shoes, freezing your miserable
  soul…"* Fragmented, sensory, present-feeling. Reserve for peaks; it fatigues if
  sustained.

**Advanced techniques**

- **Free indirect style** (Wood): render thought in the narrator's third-person
  past-tense grammar but with the *character's* vocabulary and judgment — no "he
  thought," no quotes. Diagnostic: an evaluative word appears whose owner is the
  *character* — *"Ted watched the orchestra through stupid tears."* Whose word is
  "stupid"? Ted's.
  - *Reported:* "She thought the party was boring and wished she'd stayed home." →
    *Free indirect:* "God, what a deadly party. Why had she come?"
- **Gliding distance** (Gardner): move the dial gradually — pull back to enter a
  scene (L1–2), dolly in as intensity rises (L3→4→5), pull back to close. Each
  sentence sits ~one notch from its neighbor. Smooth ramps read as control.
- **Deep POV / filter removal:** cut perception verbs that re-narrate from outside.
  - *Before:* "Sarah felt her stomach sink as she realized she'd forgotten her
    purse. She saw cars filing past and heard horns honking." → *After:* "Her
    stomach sank. Her purse — still on the café table. Cars filed past, bumper to
    bumper; horns blared."

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *Head-hopping:* switching focalizers mid-scene so the reader jolts between
  interiors. → One focalizer per scene; switch only at a scene/chapter break. Test:
  if Character B's *voice* (not just facts) appears mid-scene, it's head-hopping;
  if a single consistent narrator reports B, it's legitimate omniscience.
- *Filtering:* see above — delete the verb, promote the sensation.
- *Distance whiplash:* L1→L5 inside a paragraph → insert intermediate notches or
  place the jump at a hard break so it reads as a deliberate cut.
- *Voice bleed:* narrator's Latinate abstraction inside a child's free-indirect
  thought → match diction to the focalizer at close range.
- *Accidental omniscience in limited POV:* "Unbeknownst to her, he smiled." →
  render only what the focalizer can perceive or infer.

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. Declare the contract: fix POV type and, for limited, the focalizer per scene.
   Hold it.
2. One head per scene in limited POV; change focalizer only at a break.
3. Name = distance: full name pulls far, first name mid, pronoun close.
4. Default to L3–L4 for engaged third person; ramp to L5 only at peaks; open/close
   nearer L1–L2.
5. Move one notch at a time; no L1↔L5 jumps except across a hard break.
6. Strip filter words in close/deep POV.
7. Use free indirect when you want feeling without distance: drop "he thought,"
   keep the character's idiom in the narrator's grammar.
8. In omniscience, keep one narratorial voice for all minds; don't ventriloquize
   each character's idiom in turn.
9. For unreliable narration (Booth): let the account diverge measurably from the
   work's norms, and plant cues that let the reader detect the gap.

**Self-checks** — Assign L1–L5 to each paragraph: are transitions ≤1 notch apart
unless at a break? Filter scan: count *saw/heard/felt/knew/realized*; justify or
cut. "Whose word?": is the strongest evaluative word the focalizer's (good free
indirect) or an uninvited second mind (head-hop)? Knowledge audit: any sentence
reporting what the focalizer can't know?

**Authorities** — Gardner: a five-level dial; shift it smoothly. Wood (*How
Fiction Works*): free indirect fuses author and character; the giveaway is the
character's word. Genette: who sees vs. who speaks. Le Guin: six POVs, each with
distinct powers; switch only at structural breaks. Booth (*The Rhetoric of
Fiction*): a narrator is unreliable when they deviate from the implied author's
norms. Deep-POV consensus (Hardy, Darwin): cut filter words to collapse the gap.

## 7. Punctuation Infrastructure

**What it is.** Punctuation — and paragraphing and white space — as a control
system for pace, breath, emphasis, and structure, not merely a matter of
correctness.

**Why it matters.** "If words are the flesh and muscle of writing, then
punctuation is the breath" (Dreyer). Marks are a graduated braking system; they
tell the reader how to *hear* you (Le Guin).

**Principles**

- **Marks set pace and group ideas.** Clark's road signs: comma = speed bump,
  semicolon = rolling stop, colon = flashing yellow ("something ahead"), period =
  stop sign, dash = a branch in the road.
- **Rhythm comes from *variation*,** not any single mark. Vary length and
  stop-strength deliberately.
- **Pace is a meaning-making tool;** punctuation choices are tone choices.
- **White space is punctuation at the macro scale** — paragraph breaks and
  isolated lines are the largest, strongest pauses you have.

**Mark-by-mark function**

| Mark | Rhetorical job | Pace effect | Overuse tell |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Period .** | The hard cut; finality | Fastest; staccato in runs | Choppy, childlike monotony; no long sentences |
| **Comma ,** | Beat / breath / grouping | Slight slow | Cluttered; reader can't find the spine |
| **Semicolon ;** | Links two equal independent clauses; holds tension | Slows more than comma | Academic stiffness; chains that should be sentences |
| **Colon :** | Promise → delivery; "the curtain rises" | Forward-leaning pause | Listy, bureaucratic |
| **Em dash —** | Interruption, amplification, aside | Sharp break / acceleration | **The AI tell** — 3–5× human rate; restating without adding |
| **Parentheses ( )** | Whisper; lowers psychic volume | Drops a beat out of the line | Constant hedging; lost through-line |
| **Ellipsis …** | Trailing off, hesitation | Suspends pace | Coy, faux-thoughtful |
| **Paragraph ¶** | Macro-pause; resets attention | Largest pause | Walls of text, or strobing fragments |

**Advanced techniques**

- **Cumulative semicolon chain** (Watson): join clause after clause to build
  pressure that resolves only at the period — high-skill, sparing.
- **Colon as stage curtain:** everything before it is setup, everything after is
  payoff; the reader leans in.
- **Period-stacking for velocity:** shorten and stop hard. Short. Flat. Done.
- **Em dash as a single spotlight:** one per sentence, to *amplify* (add something
  new), never to restate.
- **Minimalist punctuation** (McCarthy: "periods, capitals, the occasional comma,
  and that's it"): buys a clean, urgent surface, but shifts the burden of clarity
  onto syntax — you must guide who's speaking through phrasing alone.
- **White-space emphasis:** give one short sentence its own paragraph; the
  surrounding silence amplifies it.

**Anti-patterns → fixes**

- *AI em-dash flood* → cap at ~1 per sentence and ~1 per paragraph; convert
  dash-joined clauses to periods, dash-introduced examples to colons, dash-asides
  to commas/parentheses. Every dash must add new information.
- *Comma splice* (two independent clauses, comma only) → period, semicolon, colon,
  or conjunction (allowed only for short parallel clauses: "He came, he saw, he
  conquered").
- *Semicolon misuse* → confirm both sides are independent clauses of roughly equal
  weight; if the relationship is forward-pointing, use a colon.
- *Period monotony* → interleave one long flowing sentence to reset breath.
- *Wall-of-text / strobe paragraphing* → break on beat/idea/speaker; reserve
  one-line paragraphs for true emphasis.

**Re-punctuation micro-examples (same content, different effect)**

- *Fast:* "She opened the door. It was empty. She knew."
- *Flowing:* "She opened the door, and it was empty, and she knew."
- *Dread:* "She opened the door; it was empty; she knew."
- *Drama (dash):* "He'd lost everything — the house, the car, the dog."
- *Promise (colon):* "He'd lost everything: the house, the car, the dog."
- *Whisper (parens):* "He'd lost everything (the house, the car, even the dog)."

**Operational rules (checkable)**

1. Cap em dashes: ≤1 per sentence, ~≤1 per paragraph; each must add new info.
2. No comma splices (except short parallel clauses).
3. Semicolon test: both sides independent, equal weight, closely related.
4. Colon test: what follows must deliver on what precedes.
5. Vary stop-strength: don't let every sentence in a paragraph end the same way.
6. One emphasis device per beat — don't stack dash + italics + exclamation +
   one-line paragraph on the same point.
7. Match register: tense passages → periods and short sentences; reflective →
   commas/semicolons extending breath.
8. Reserve isolated one-line paragraphs for the single most important beat.
9. Budget: ≤1 parenthetical and ≤1 ellipsis per ~300 words unless dialogue
   demands more.

**Self-checks** — Em-dash count: >~1 per 200–250 words is the AI tell. Read aloud
for breath: out of air = missing mark; robotic = uniform sentences. Stop-strength
map: all periods? add flow. Splice sweep. Promise audit on every colon. Restate
check: does any dash/parenthesis merely repeat the prior clause? Delete.

**Authorities** — Dreyer (*Dreyer's English*): punctuation is the breath. Watson
(*Semicolon*): the semicolon is an instrument of balance and tension. Casagrande
(*The Best Punctuation Book, Period*): decide on purpose, not superstition. Clark
(*The Glamour of Grammar*): marks are road signs that set pace. Lukeman (*A Dash of
Style*): chase rhythm and pacing; build a symphony of marks. Le Guin: punctuation
tells the reader how to hear you. McCarthy: minimal marks buy urgency at the cost
of putting clarity on syntax.

## 8. The master revision pass

Run these gates in order on any draft. Each maps to a module above.

1. **Architecture.** Every scene-to-scene link is *but/therefore*, not *and then*.
   Every scene turns a value. Setups have payoffs; the climax tests the *need*.
2. **Perspective.** POV contract declared and held. One head per scene. Distance
   levels assigned; transitions ≤1 notch except at breaks. Filter words stripped
   in close POV.
3. **Subtext & Exposition.** No character names a feeling or states shared
   knowledge. Every exposed fact is wanted and staked. Longest info block ≤3
   sentences without friction. Subtext yields to text only at the rupture.
4. **Prose.** Cut every removable word. One strong verb per sentence. Concrete
   over abstract. Strongest word last. No hedge/inflation set.
5. **Cadence.** Length varies (no 3 consecutive within ±3 words; a short after any
   long). Sentences and paragraphs end on strong words. No accidental meter.
6. **Punctuation.** Em dashes ≤1/paragraph and additive. No comma splices.
   Stop-strength varies. White space earns its pauses.
7. **Read it aloud** one final time. Fix the first place the ear flinches; repeat
   until it doesn't.

**The two questions that govern all seven:** *Does this earn the reader's
attention right here?* and *Have I trusted the reader to do their share?*
