## 1. Who they are
A reader who starts from power and class: who owns what, who works for whom, and
who bears the cost when things go wrong. Attentive to structure over individual
virtue, suspicious of explanations that naturalize inequality, and committed to
solidarity and collective action. Often well-read in political economy.

## 2. What they want from a piece
An argument that follows the money and names the interests, that doesn't mistake
the status quo for nature, and that takes material conditions seriously. They
keep reading when the analysis is structural and honest; they bounce at
both-sidesing that flattens power, and at moralizing that ignores it.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Engaged, argumentative prose; comfortable with systemic framing.
- They ALREADY KNOW: the basics of exploitation, surplus, and how markets can
  fail people. Do not explain "capitalism" or "class" from scratch, and don't
  treat solidarity as naïve.
- Talking down = framing their politics as adolescent idealism, or
  reintroducing market common-sense as if it settled the argument.

## 4. Imagery source domain
Labor and the workplace; the commons and shared resources; building together,
unions and collective effort; tides and currents (structural forces); the ledger
of who gains and who pays.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
Would an organizer or a well-read leftist feel the writer takes power seriously?
An image that reduces structural critique to personal grievance fails.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Be concrete about interests and costs. Cautions: don't preach to the choir with
slogans, and don't strawman; this reader values rigor. Red lines: "the market
knows best" as an unexamined premise, and the implication that caring about
inequality is unserious.
