# _template.md — audience profile schema (with worked example)

*Copy this file to create a new audience. Fill all six sections. Save as
`<audience-id>.md` (lowercase, hyphens) in `_config/audiences/`. A template
plus the filled example below is executable by a much dumber model than a
template alone — so the example is part of the contract, not decoration.*

---

## The schema

```
# <audience-id>.md — <one-line description>

## 1. Who they are        a sentence or two; the real person, not a demographic cell
## 2. What they want from a piece   why they're reading; what "worth it" means to them
## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
##    - reading level
##    - WHAT THEY ALREADY KNOW (the writer must never re-explain this)
##    - what counts as talking DOWN to them, specifically
## 4. Their world (for imagery)   the source domain a controlling metaphor is drawn from
## 5. Heat and humour      how much provocation/irony/meme lands; what offends
## 6. Lines not to cross    hard vetoes — topics, framings, or tones that lose them
```

Section 3 is the load-bearing one: it is how the respect floor in `rules.md`
gets teeth. "What they already know" is a *prohibition list* for re-explanation.

---

## Worked example — `cyclist.md`

```
# cyclist.md — the committed urban cyclist

## 1. Who they are
Rides for transport, not sport. Knows the city by its road surfaces and its
near-misses. Reads anything about infrastructure, road law, or car culture with
a stake in it.

## 2. What they want from a piece
To feel the writer actually rides — not a journalist's tourist take. A real
claim, evidence they can use in an argument, no false balance that treats
"cyclists vs drivers" as symmetric.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Reading level: educated general; comfortable with policy and stats.
- Already knows (do NOT re-explain): what a protected lane is, why dooring
  happens, that helmets are politically loaded, the basic geometry of why trucks
  kill at junctions.
- Talking down to them = explaining that "cycling is good exercise," or framing
  riders as a hobby group rather than commuters.

## 4. Their world (for imagery)
Roads, flow, friction, desire lines, the physics of momentum and braking, the
network as a circulatory system.

## 5. Heat and humour
High tolerance for sharp, even partisan takes on car dependency. Dark humour
about near-misses lands. Memes about infrastructure (bollards, "one more lane")
land if format permits.

## 6. Lines not to cross
Don't both-sides road deaths. Don't moralise about helmet/hi-vis as if safety is
the rider's failure. Don't conflate sport cycling with transport cycling.
```

*Notice how §3 forbids re-explanation and §6 lists framings that lose the reader
outright. Every new audience needs both filled, concretely.*
