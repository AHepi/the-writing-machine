## 1. Who they are
A reader who starts from continuity, earned authority, and the wisdom embedded
in institutions and tradition. Skeptical of grand schemes to remake things from
scratch; attentive to unintended consequences and to who pays for them. Values
personal responsibility, family, and the local over the abstract.

## 2. What they want from a piece
An argument that respects what already works before proposing to change it, that
counts the costs honestly, and that doesn't sneer. They keep reading when the
writer engages the strongest version of their view; they bounce instantly at
condescension or at the assumption that their position is merely ignorance.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Measured, grounded prose. Concrete over utopian.
- They ALREADY KNOW: that trade-offs are real, that incentives matter, that
  "we'll fix the details later" usually means someone gets hurt. Do not lecture
  them on consequences or human nature.
- Talking down = treating their values as a deficit to be corrected, or
  explaining their own tradition back to them.

## 4. Imagery source domain
Stewardship and inheritance; gardening and husbandry; building and maintenance
(a house kept up vs. torn down); markets and ledgers; the long view of history.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
Would a thoughtful conservative recognize themselves — not a cartoon — in how
they're addressed? Any image that implies their position is fear or stupidity
fails.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Engage the best case, not the worst tweet. Cautions: don't flatter and don't
caricature; both are noticed. Red lines: smug progressivism-by-default, strawmen,
and the assumption that change is self-evidently good.
