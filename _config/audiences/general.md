# general.md — the smart layperson (default audience)

*The default. When the brief names no audience, this is who the piece is for.
The smart layperson is the workspace's baseline of respect: curious, capable,
busy, and never to be talked down to.*

## 1. Who they are

An intelligent adult reading outside their own specialty. They didn't study this
topic but they reason well, hold a job that demands judgment, and have read a
good newspaper or a long magazine piece before. New to the subject; not new to
thinking.

## 2. What they want from a piece

To understand something they couldn't before, fast and without being
patronised. They'll give you their attention if you spend it on the subject and
not on throat-clearing. "Worth it" means they can now explain the core idea to
someone else.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge

- **Reading level:** educated general — comfortable with a long sentence, a
  named concept, a number with context. No jargon without a first naming.
- **Already knows (do NOT re-explain):** how the everyday world works — basic
  economics (supply, demand, incentives), basic science (cause and effect,
  rough scales), how institutions and politics broadly function, how to read a
  percentage. Assume general literacy and numeracy.
- **Talking down to them =** defining words any adult knows, over-signposting
  ("as we will see"), padding obvious points, or treating a first encounter with
  the *topic* as a first encounter with *thought*.

## 4. Their world (for imagery)

Shared everyday life: cooking, traffic, weather, money, household repair, sport,
common workplaces. Metaphors drawn from things nearly everyone has done.

## 5. Heat and humour

Moderate by default. Dry wit lands; partisanship and in-group memes mostly
don't, because this audience is broad. Let `provocation` raise the heat only
when the brief asks.

## 6. Lines not to cross

- Don't assume a political tribe — this audience spans them.
- Don't flatter or pander; respect is shown by clarity, not by agreement.
- Don't bury the point under context. State it, then earn it.
