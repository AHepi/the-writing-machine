# The Ground Rent We All Pay

When a train line opens in a New Zealand suburb, the land beside it gets richer
overnight. Not the workers who laid the track, not the tenants who will ride it
to work, not the council that borrowed to build it — the person who owns the dirt.
He dug nothing, built nothing, risked nothing. The value that flooded into his
section was produced by everyone around him, and under the present arrangement he
keeps every cent of it. That is not a quirk of an overheated market. It is the
oldest engine of accumulation we have, and a land value tax is the most direct
lever ever designed against it.

Start with what the tax actually reaches, because the name is modest and the
target is not. A land value tax falls on the unimproved worth of the ground —
what the bare site alone would sell for — and leaves the building on top
untouched. Put two sections side by side: one carries flats full of tenants, the
other is held empty by a speculator waiting for the price to climb. Under this
tax they owe exactly the same, because the land beneath them is worth the same.
The tax does not touch what you make. It touches what you merely hold.

## Rent is a social product, privately pocketed

The case begins where any honest account of land begins — with the fact that
ground rent is not earned. The worth of a location is produced collectively: by
the public infrastructure run to it, by the labour and custom of everyone who
lives and works nearby, by the simple density of a society pressing in around a
fixed point. None of it is made by the owner. He collects it the way a tollkeeper
collects at a bridge he did not build. A land value tax returns that
socially-created value to the society that created it, and — this is the part
that matters — it does so without blunting anyone's incentive to actually build,
because it leaves the building alone.

You do not need to be a radical to see this; the founders of the discipline saw
it first. Adam Smith noted that a tax on ground rent creates no waste and cannot
be passed on to tenants, because the landlord is already charging all the market
will bear. The point survived into modern economics under a colder name: because
the supply of land is fixed, a tax on its value causes almost none of the
deadweight that taxes on wages or production cause — you cannot manufacture less
land to dodge the bill. Tax labour and you get a little less labour. Tax capital
and it books its profits in a friendlier jurisdiction. Tax land and it sits
exactly where it is, because a quarter-acre cannot be relocated to the Cayman
Islands. Of everything a state can reach for, this is the one base that neither
flees nor punishes work — far more efficient than the taxes that fall on the
people who do the producing.

So we have a value nobody earned, a tax nobody can escape, and a burden that lands
on accumulation rather than on labour. The only question left is why a country
would refuse it. New Zealand has refused it harder than most — and the refusal has
a shape worth naming.

## A country that chose to tax work and spare wealth

New Zealand is nearly alone in the developed world in having no broad tax on
capital gains. Sell shares, sell a rental, sell a paddock that tripled in value
while you slept, and the gain is generally yours untouched — caught only by the
narrow bright-line test, a two-year window that nets the clumsiest residential
flips and lets the rest sail through. Meanwhile the wage earner is taxed at source,
every fortnight, with nowhere to hide a pay packet. This is a settlement, not an
accident: a tax system arranged so that income from owning is treated more gently
than income from working.

The predictable thing happened. With the most lightly-taxed return in the country
sitting in land, the surplus flowed there. Housing stopped being shelter and
became the national asset class — the place a propertied generation parks its
money and watches it compound. The figures are not subtle.

By 2024 the median New
Zealand house cost roughly **14.6 times** the median household's yearly income;
in Auckland the ratio has run between eight and eleven. The international rule of
thumb calls housing affordable at three times income or below. A whole generation
has been priced out of the most basic security there is, while the value of the
ground beneath the people who got in early climbs untaxed. That is what untaxed
rent does: it transfers, quietly and continuously, from those who must pay for
access to those who already hold it.

## We already had the machine — and dismantled it

Here is the part that should sting. The usual objection to a land tax everywhere
else is practical — you cannot value the land apart from the building, it is too
hard, too contestable. In New Zealand that objection collapses on contact.
Councils already rate property, and the law lets them rate on land value if they
choose; the land component of every property in the country is revalued on a
three-year cycle. Every section already carries an official Land Value, recorded
separately from the building. The meter is installed and turning.

And we are not breaking new ground — we are returning to it. New Zealand ran a
national land tax until **1992**, when it was abolished. The movement to tax land
values bit deeper here than almost anywhere on earth; proposals to rate the land
and spare the buildings surfaced in these provinces as early as 1849. This is not
a foreign import. It is a domestic inheritance that was given away, and the giving
away was a choice that served the people holding land. The idea is stirring again
from below: in 2024 Wellington City Council was openly weighing a switch to rating
on land value, and even the government's own 2019 Tax Working Group conceded that
a land tax was well suited to being levied by councils. The plumbing is in. Only
the political will is missing — and we know whose interests its absence protects.

## The objection that deserves an honest answer

There is one objection that must be taken at full strength, because it is true and
because it is also the one most often raised to shut the conversation down. Picture
the widow in the villa she and her husband bought in 1975, now worth two million
dollars through no act of hers — the suburb rose around her. Her income is the
pension. A tax on her land's soaring value is a bill she cannot pay. Picture the
farmer whose whole balance sheet is dirt and stock, asset-rich and cash-poor. Tax
these people on wealth they cannot spend and you can drive them from the home and
the farm. The cost is real, and it carries real weight at the ballot box.

It deserves a real answer, not a wave of the hand — and New Zealand built the
answer before it needed it. Councils already run rates postponement schemes: an
eligible owner, typically over sixty-five or on a low income, can stop paying and
let the bill accumulate against the property, settled with interest only when it
is finally sold or passes through the estate. Apply that to a land tax and the
widow keeps her villa. She pays nothing from her pension; the tax is drawn from
the windfall at the moment the windfall becomes real money. Deferral is a standard,
proven tool, not an improvisation rigged up to rescue the idea. And notice what it
does and does not protect: it shelters the pensioner sitting in a home she lives
in, not the investor who is "cash-poor" only because his wealth is parked in land
he is waiting to flip. The hard case is met; the speculator's borrowed sympathy is
not.

Concede the rest plainly, because solidarity is not the same as dishonesty: uptake
of these schemes is low today, and people hate the thought of debt stacking up
against the family home. A serious land tax would have to make deferral generous
and close to automatic, not a grudging form buried on a council website. But that
is a problem of design, and design is something we control. It is not a reason to
leave the lever on the floor.

## Whose value, returned to whom

Put it together and the shape is hard to argue with on the merits — which is
precisely why the argument is rarely allowed to happen on the merits. A tax almost
nobody can dodge, because land cannot move. A tax cheap to collect, because the
rails are already laid and the meters already turn. A tax that makes hoarding an
empty section while the city strains for housing genuinely costly, and that asks
nothing of the person who actually builds. For a country that spent a generation
treating land as the best place to store wealth rather than the place to house
people, that is not a punishment. It is a correction.

The train line opens, the suburb fills, the corner gets rich. The only question a
land value tax asks is the one the present arrangement is built to avoid: the value
was made by all of us, so why does one of us get to keep it? Return the rent to the
town that produced it. New Zealand, almost uniquely, already owns the means to do
it — and once did.
