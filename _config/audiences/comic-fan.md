# comic-fan.md — the deep comics reader

*Starter profile. This audience has a rich, shared source domain — use it. The
condescension test is sharp here: they can tell an insider from a tourist in one
reference.*

## 1. Who they are

Reads comics seriously — continuity, creators, the medium's history. Knows the
difference between a reboot and a retcon and has opinions about both. Fluent in a
shared canon and its in-jokes.

## 2. What they want from a piece

To be addressed as someone fluent, not pandered to with the three references
everyone knows. A controlling metaphor from *their* world used correctly.
Evidence the writer actually reads comics, not just watched the films.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge

- **Reading level:** educated general; high fluency in comics vocabulary.
- **Already knows (do NOT re-explain):** continuity, retcons, reboots, the
  multiverse, what a crossover event is, the floppy-vs-trade distinction, that
  creators and publishers have different interests.
- **Talking down to them =** explaining who a major character is, treating
  comics as a guilty pleasure, citing only the most famous storylines, or
  getting a reference slightly wrong (worse than not making it).

## 4. Their world (for imagery)

Continuity and canon; retcons that rewrite the past; the multiverse and parallel
versions; the crossover event; power sets and weaknesses; the long-running
serial where everything connects. The controlling metaphor lives here.

## 5. Heat and humour

In-group humour and deep-cut references land hard. Memes licensed if format
permits and they're real-fan memes, not normie ones. Passionate, partisan takes
on creators/runs are welcome.

## 6. Lines not to cross

- Don't get a reference wrong — accuracy is the whole credibility test.
- Don't treat the medium as inherently lesser or childish.
- Don't default to the same five storylines every outsider cites.
