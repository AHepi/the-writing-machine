# brief.md — a from-zero crash course in constructor theory

topic:        constructor theory — the proposal by David Deutsch and Chiara Marletto to rewrite fundamental physics in terms of which transformations are possible, which are impossible, and why
goal:         a reader with no maths or physics can explain what constructor theory is, how it differs from the standard "predict the future from initial conditions and laws of motion" picture, what its core ideas are (tasks, constructors, the possible/impossible divide, counterfactuals), what it has been used for so far (information, life, thermodynamics), and — honestly — what is settled versus still open

audience:     beginner
format:       textbook
length:       6,000–9,000 words
visuals:      auto
provocation:  none
audit:        on
notes:        Assume zero maths and zero physics; define every term and every symbol in plain words on first use. Be honest that constructor theory is a young, not-yet-mainstream research programme, not established physics. Render via the tufte-pdf skill (margin sidenotes, clean math, boxed "math, gently" asides).

deliverables:
- D1: a title block — a title, a one-line subtitle, and a short "what you'll be able to do by the end" promise
- D2: chapter 1 opens on a concrete everyday puzzle and names the single question the whole course answers
- D3: explains, in plain terms, the standard ("prevailing") way physics works — predicting what will happen from initial conditions plus laws of motion — before introducing the alternative
- D4: explains constructor theory's reframing — physics stated as which tasks are possible vs impossible, and why that is a different kind of law
- D5: defines the load-bearing terms (task, constructor, substrate, possible task, impossible task) in plain words, with any notation introduced gently and every symbol named
- D6: explains what a counterfactual is and why "the science of can and can't" puts can/can't statements at the foundation
- D7: walks through the worked subsidiary theories built so far — information (2015), life (2015), thermodynamics — at full density, each grounded in an everyday example
- D8: a closing chapter that is honest about status: what the programme claims, what (if anything) is testable (the gravity-entanglement proposal), and that it is early-stage and not yet mainstream
- D9: a glossary defining every term introduced
- D10: a further-reading list pointing to real, F-tagged sources
- D11: every chapter built to full density — the last chapter as carefully as the first; no compressed or skipped sections
- D12: length 6,000–9,000 words
