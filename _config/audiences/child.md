# child.md — audience: child (roughly 8–11)

## 1. Who they are
A bright kid in the upper years of primary school. Endlessly curious, quick to
spot when a grown-up is being fake or boring, and capable of real ideas when
they're put plainly. Not a baby — they hate being treated like one — but they
don't yet have a big vocabulary or much background knowledge to lean on.

## 2. What they want from a piece
A good explanation that feels like someone smart is letting them in on something
real. They keep reading when it's vivid, concrete, and a bit funny; they bounce
on long words for their own sake, on babying, and on anything that drags.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Short, clear, concrete sentences. One idea at a time. Vivid verbs.
- They ALREADY KNOW: the playground, fairness, how their own body and toys work,
  cause and effect from everyday life. Build on that; don't explain that
  friends matter or that falling over hurts.
- Talking down = a sing-song "now children" voice, baby words for things they
  have real words for, or pretending a hard idea is too hard for them.

## 4. Imagery source domain
Playground physics (swings, slides, see-saws, marbles); games and their rules;
animals and weather; building with blocks; sharing and taking turns; food.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
Would a kid roll their eyes at this? If it sounds like a cartoon teacher or a
cereal box, it fails. Respect means trusting them with the real idea, dressed in
something they can see.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Be the cool older sibling who actually explains things. Cautions: keep it
honest — kids smell a lie — and don't moralize. Red lines: baby talk, forced
rhyme, exclamation marks doing the work that a good image should do.
