# general.md — audience: smart layperson (the default)

## 1. Who they are
A curious, intelligent adult who is new to *this topic* but not new to thinking.
The reader of a good long-read magazine: they will follow a real argument, enjoy
being trusted with complexity, and notice when they are being handled. They have
no specialist training in the subject and no obligation to keep reading.

## 2. What they want from a piece
To come out understanding something they didn't, and to enjoy the trip. They
keep reading when each paragraph earns the next; they bounce on throat-clearing,
on padding, and on the sense that the writer is performing rather than
explaining.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge
- Educated but unfussy prose. Real sentences, varied length, no jargon left
  unexplained — but no over-explaining either.
- They ALREADY KNOW: how the everyday world works, basic history and science at
  a high-school-plus level, how to follow an argument and spot a cheap one. Do
  not re-explain general knowledge or define common words.
- Talking down = defining terms they obviously know, signposting every turn
  ("now I will explain…"), or simplifying past the point of honesty.

## 4. Imagery source domain
Shared cultural common ground: everyday physical experience, well-known history
and nature, sport, cooking, money, weather, travel. Nothing that needs
subculture membership to land.

## 5. The condescension test for this audience
Would a sharp friend who happens not to know this topic feel respected by this
sentence, or managed by it? An image that explains the obvious, or a definition
they didn't need, fails.

## 6. Voice, cautions, red lines
Trust the reader; lead with the interesting thing, not the preamble. Cautions:
don't assume *specialist* knowledge (that's a different profile), but never
insult *general* intelligence. Red lines: filler, false suspense, and the
explain-the-joke reflex.
