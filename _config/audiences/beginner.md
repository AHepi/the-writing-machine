# beginner.md — the curious adult, no maths or physics

*An adult mind with an empty toolbox for this subject. New to the topic is not
new to thinking: assume sharp everyday reasoning and zero technical vocabulary.
The respect floor does the heavy lifting — write up to a clever friend who never
took the course, never down to a student who must be managed.*

## 1. Who they are

A capable adult — runs a life, holds a job, follows an argument — who simply
never learned this field. No calculus, no physics, no notation. They picked this
up because they got curious, and they will close it the moment it either
patronises them or loses them in jargon. They are not stupid; they are
un-briefed.

## 2. What they want from a piece

To actually understand, not to be made to feel they understand. "Worth it" =
they can explain the core idea to someone else over coffee and get it roughly
right. They want the real thing made reachable — not a watered-down cartoon that
falls apart the moment they poke it.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge

- **Reading level:** plain, warm, unhurried. One new idea per paragraph. Every
  technical term, symbol, or piece of notation is defined in ordinary words the
  first time it appears — no exceptions. If an equation must appear, every part
  of it is named.
- **Already knows (do NOT re-explain):** everyday cause and effect; that
  machines and recipes have steps; how rules work in games; how to follow an
  analogy; that "it depends" is sometimes the honest answer. They reason fine —
  they just lack the vocabulary and the facts of this field.
- **Talking down to them =** the words "simply," "obviously," "of course," or
  "just"; fake cheerleading ("the fun part!"); explaining that learning is good
  for you; or pretending a hard idea is easy when it isn't. Treat difficulty
  honestly: "this next bit is genuinely strange — here's a handle on it."

## 4. Their world (for imagery)

The ordinary built world: kitchens and recipes, machines and factories, tools
and repair, building and fixing, games with rules, money and trading, libraries
and copying. A controlling metaphor is drawn from here — something they could
touch — and extended honestly, never past its breaking point.

## 5. Heat and humour

Light, warm wit lands; dry understatement lands. Snark, irony aimed at the
reader, and in-group cleverness do not. No political "provocation" — heat here
means delight and the small shock of a counter-intuitive truth, not edge.

## 6. Lines not to cross

- Never use a term, symbol, or formula without first saying what it means in
  plain words.
- Never assume a step is "obvious" — if it took the field a century to see it, it
  is not obvious to a reader on page three.
- Never fake-simplify into something false. A reader who later learns the truth
  should feel they were given a true seed, not a comforting lie.
- Never condescend, moralise, or perform enthusiasm. Respect the reader's
  intelligence exactly as much as you fill in their knowledge.
