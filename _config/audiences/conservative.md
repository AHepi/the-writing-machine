# conservative.md — the thoughtful conservative reader

*Starter profile — written to the template so the system runs end to end. Strike
what doesn't sound right to you and rewrite §3 and §6 in your own read of this
audience; those two sections carry the most weight.*

## 1. Who they are

Reads to test an argument against settled commitments, not to have priors
flattered. Values institutions that have survived, responsibility over
intention, and proof over aspiration. Sceptical of novelty sold as progress.

## 2. What they want from a piece

An argument that takes their commitments seriously enough to engage them, not
caricature them. Concrete consequences over abstract ideals. Respect for trade-
offs — they distrust any case that claims no cost.

## 3. Register and assumed knowledge

- **Reading level:** educated general to high; comfortable with history,
  institutions, and economic reasoning.
- **Already knows (do NOT re-explain):** how markets and incentives work, why
  unintended consequences happen, the value of precedent and slow change, basic
  civics and constitutional structure.
- **Talking down to them =** treating their position as ignorance to be cured,
  assuming they haven't heard the progressive case, or moralising rather than
  arguing.

## 4. Their world (for imagery)

Stewardship, inheritance, the household and its budget, gardening and
cultivation, institutions as buildings maintained across generations, debts paid
and debts owed.

## 5. Heat and humour

Wry, understated humour over snark. Heat works when it's aimed at hypocrisy or
folly, not at the reader's tribe. `provocation: high` should sharpen the
argument, never sneer.

## 6. Lines not to cross

- Don't strawman religious or traditional commitments as mere prejudice.
- Don't assume bad faith; assume a different weighting of real goods.
- Don't pretend change is costless — they will not believe you.
