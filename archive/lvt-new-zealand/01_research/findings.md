## Serving D3 — what LVT is (the mechanism, with a NZ concrete)

- **F1:** A land value tax falls on the *unimproved* value of land — what the
  bare site would sell for — and not on the buildings or improvements on it.
  Two identical adjacent lots owe the same tax whether one is a vacant section
  and the other holds a four-storey apartment block. [src: LocalHousingSolutions; Sightline] — *medium*
- **F2:** In New Zealand, every property already carries an official **Land
  Value (LV)** figure — "the likely price that just the land (minus buildings
  and improvements) would sell for" — recorded separately from Capital Value
  (CV) in the council rating valuation. [src: QV; BullerDC] — *strong (official definition)*

## Serving D1 — the efficiency thesis (why land is the ideal tax base)

- **F3:** Because the supply of land is fixed (inelastic), a tax on pure land
  value creates no deadweight loss — owners cannot make less land in response,
  so the tax does not distort production the way taxes on labour or capital do.
  This is a long-standing and broad consensus in public-finance economics. [src: ChicagoFed; IMF-WP-2022] — *strong*
- **F4:** Land rent is widely treated as a *collectively created* value — it
  rises with location, public infrastructure and the surrounding community's
  activity rather than the owner's own effort — so taxing it returns to the
  community value the community created, without blunting the incentive to
  produce. [src: ChicagoFed; arXiv-2511.21766] — *medium (synthesis)*
- **F5:** Adam Smith already noted that a land value tax does not create
  deadweight losses and cannot be passed on to tenants. (Economic incidence
  falls on the landowner because supply can't shrink.) [src: ChicagoFed] — *medium (historical attribution)*
- **F6:** LVT discourages land banking and speculation: because the tax is owed
  whether or not the site is developed, holding vacant or under-used land
  becomes costly, pushing owners to build or sell rather than sit on it for
  capital gain. [src: Sightline; LincolnInst; LocalHousingSolutions] — *medium*

## Serving D4 — New Zealand-specific conditions

- **F7:** New Zealand housing is among the least affordable in the developed
  world. As of the June 2024 year, the median house sale price was about
  $753,500 against median household equivalised disposable income of about
  $51,597 — roughly **14.6 times income** nationally; Auckland's price-to-income
  ratio has run around 8–11x. (Demographia's "affordable" threshold is 3.0.) [src: StatsNZ; Demographia2024; interest.co.nz] — *strong (official + dataset)*
- **F8:** New Zealand has **no comprehensive capital gains tax**; gains on land
  are caught only by narrow rules such as the bright-line test (taxing
  residential sales within a set window of purchase — 2 years as of 2024). [src: BDO; IRD; WK] — *strong (statutory)*
- **F9:** The 2019 Tax Working Group declined to recommend a broad land tax for
  central government but explicitly noted a **land tax could be levied by
  councils as a local tax**; its main recommendation (a broad CGT) was not
  adopted. New Zealand has implemented neither. [src: TWG-FinalReport; austaxpolicy; VUW] — *strong (official report)*
- **F10:** NZ councils already assess and rate property and may legally set
  rates on **land value, capital value, or annual value**; historically many
  councils rated on land/unimproved value, and the rating system is maintained
  by revaluation at least every three years under the Rating Valuations Act
  1998. [src: TeAra-Taxes; QV; BullerDC] — *strong (statute + encyclopedia)*
- **F11:** The Henry George "single tax on the unearned increment of land"
  movement "had more effect in New Zealand than most places"; proposals to rate
  land excluding improvements appeared in Wellington and Marlborough as early as
  1849. New Zealand also levied a **national land tax until it was abolished in
  1992** (alongside estate duty). [src: TeAra-EconThought; TeAra-Taxes; ExpatFocus] — *medium-to-strong*
- **F12 (current):** In 2024 **Wellington City Council was actively considering
  switching its rates to a land-value basis** — a live, present-day NZ debate
  about taxing land value rather than total property value. [src: Spinoff-Windbag] — *medium (reputable reporting)*

## Serving D2 — the strongest counterargument and its answer (asset-rich, cash-poor)

- **F13 (the objection):** The standard objection to LVT is that it hits the
  asset-rich but cash-poor — retirees on fixed incomes and farmers whose wealth
  is locked in land — who may owe tax they cannot pay from current income. [src: LessWrong; TruthOnMarket] — *medium (states the objection)*
- **F14 (the answer, NZ-specific):** New Zealand already runs the mechanism that
  defuses this: **rates postponement schemes**. Eligible owners (typically 65+,
  or low-income) can defer rates for life, with the accrued amount plus interest
  repaid only when the property is sold or settled from the estate. The govt.nz
  service and many councils (e.g. Queenstown-Lakes, Napier, Invercargill) offer
  it. [src: Govt.nz-defer; QLDC; Napier; OAG-2006] — *strong (official scheme)*
- **F15 (the answer, general):** Deferral of land/property tax for asset-rich,
  cash-poor owners is an established policy tool internationally (use-value and
  deferral programmes for farm and forest land, payment-spreading for farmland),
  so the cash-flow problem is treated as solvable by design, not fatal to LVT. [src: Albemarle; Multnomah; Instead] — *medium (overseas examples)*

## Contested / caveats (flagged honestly for stage 02)

- **C1:** Accurately isolating the "unimproved" land value is genuinely hard —
  Te Ara notes it "proved difficult to identify the unearned increment," and a
  pure land tax historically struggled to raise enough revenue alone. The draft
  must not claim valuation is trivial. NZ's existing LV figures mitigate but do
  not eliminate this. [src: TeAra-EconThought; TeAra-Taxes] — *contested vs F2/F10*
- **C2:** "No deadweight loss" holds for a tax on *pure* land value; real
  assessments are imperfect, so the real-world efficiency gain is large but not
  literally perfect. The draft should claim "far more efficient," not "costless."
  [src: NBER-w8913; Springer] — *caveat on F3*
- **C3:** The cash-poor objection is real enough that it has political force even
  where deferral exists (uptake of postponement is low; people dislike debt
  against the family home). Answer it, don't wave it away. [src: Retirement.govt.nz-research] — *caveat on F14*
