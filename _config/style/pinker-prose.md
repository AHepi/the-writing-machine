# pinker-prose.md — the prose floor (classic style)

The delivery standard for every piece this workspace produces, after Steven
Pinker's account of *classic style* in *The Sense of Style*. This is the long
form; `pinker-core.md` is the ≤350-token distillation that travels in tight
prompts. When they seem to disagree, this file governs.

## The governing metaphor: prose is a window

Classic style models writing as **showing**, not telling, arguing, or
self-display. The writer has seen something in the world and angles a clear pane
of glass so the reader can see it too. Good prose draws no attention to itself;
the reader should forget there is prose at all and simply see the thing. Every
rule below follows from this one image.

## The stance: a conversation between equals

The writer is not a lecturer above the reader, nor a bureaucrat hedging against
complaint. The writer is a knowledgeable peer directing the gaze of an equal who
simply hasn't seen this view yet. This is the structural source of the workspace's
respect floor (`rules.md §respect-floor`): condescension is impossible in classic
style by construction, because the reader is presumed as capable as the writer.

## What this rules out

- **Metadiscourse.** Don't narrate the prose. "In this section I will argue…",
  "Having shown X, we now turn to Y." Show; don't announce the showing.
- **Hedging clutter.** "It could perhaps be argued that in some sense…" Commit.
  If a claim needs a caveat, give the caveat as content, not as a nervous tic.
- **Professional narcissism.** Don't write about your discipline's debates when
  the reader came to see the world. The topic is the thing, not the field's
  conversation about the thing.
- **Nominalizations and the passive as default.** Prefer people doing things.
  "The decision was made" hides who decided; classic style shows the actor.
- **Clichés and zombie nouns.** Fresh, concrete words for concrete things.

## What it asks for

- **Concrete over abstract.** Show a particular case the reader can picture; let
  the abstraction be inferred from the image.
- **A confident, direct voice.** State things. The pane is clear when the writer
  isn't fogging it with self-protection.
- **Coherence the reader can feel.** Each sentence connects to the last; the
  reader is never asked to hold an unexplained gap. (But don't over-signpost —
  trust the reader to follow a clean line.)
- **The reader's eye, led.** Arrange so that what comes first sets up what comes
  next; given-before-new; the heavy thing at the end of the sentence.

## The curse of knowledge — the chief enemy

The single biggest reason good thinkers write badly: they forget what it is like
not to already know the thing. They use jargon as a reflex, skip the step that is
obvious to them, and abbreviate the chain of reasoning. The cure is not
dumbing-down (that violates the respect floor) but **un-forgetting the reader's
starting point**: name the thing before its abbreviation, show the step you'd
skip, make the implicit chain visible. Each audience profile's §3 tells you
exactly where that reader's starting point is.

## How this composes with the rest of the workspace

- The audience profile sets the reader's assumed knowledge; classic style sets
  the posture toward that reader.
- `imagery.md` governs the metaphors that do the *showing* this style depends on.
- `persuasion.md` adds the moves for pieces that argue, without abandoning the
  window — you persuade by letting the reader see, not by raising your voice.
- `rules.md` turns the style's implications into checkable law.
