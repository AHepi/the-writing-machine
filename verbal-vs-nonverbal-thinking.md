# Thinking in Words, Thinking Without Them

Ask people to describe what goes on in their heads when they think, and you get
strikingly different reports. Some describe a running commentary — a voice
narrating, arguing, rehearsing what to say next. Others describe something almost
impossible to put into words precisely because it *isn't* made of words: a felt
sense of how parts fit together, a shape rotating, a structure clicking into
place. Both groups are reasoning. They are simply doing it with different
materials. The first is thinking verbally; the second is thinking conceptually,
or abstractly, beneath or beyond language. Understanding the difference dissolves
a surprising amount of mutual incomprehension between people who are, in fact,
both intelligent — just intelligent in different currencies.

## Two materials for thought

**Verbal thinking** uses language as its medium. Its hallmark is *inner speech* —
the silent monologue most people recognize, the sentences you "hear" without
speaking. Verbal thought is built from the properties of language: it is
sequential (one word after another), discrete (made of separable units), and
propositional (it asserts things that can be true or false). When you talk
yourself through a decision, rehearse an argument, or remember a phone number by
repeating it, you are using the verbal system. It is the natural home of logic,
explicit reasoning, instruction-following, and anything that has to be
communicated, because it is already in the form communication requires.

**Non-verbal, abstract conceptual thinking** uses something else: imagery, spatial
relationships, structures, analogies, and raw conceptual relations that have not
yet been — and may never be — translated into sentences. Here the materials are
holistic rather than sequential. A whole configuration can be present "at once,"
the way you can take in a diagram in a glance that would take a paragraph to
describe. This is the mode of the engineer who *sees* how a mechanism moves, the
mathematician who feels the symmetry of a proof before writing a line, the chess
player who recognizes a position's character without enumerating moves. The
thought is real and precise; it just doesn't arrive pre-packaged in words.

The crucial point is that these are different *formats*, not different *amounts*
of thinking. A common mistake is to assume the person without an inner monologue
is somehow thinking less, or that the person who can't summon a vivid mental image
is impaired. Neither is true. They are running thought on a different substrate.

## Verbal reasoning versus abstract conceptual reasoning

The distinction the question presses on — people who lean on *verbal reasoning*
versus those who lean on *abstract conceptual thinking* — is best understood as a
difference in default strategy for solving problems.

The **verbal reasoner** attacks a problem by talking it through, internally or
aloud. They externalize structure into propositions and then operate on those
propositions: *if this, then that; but if that, then this can't hold.* Their
strength is articulation. Because their thinking is already language-shaped, they
can explain their reasoning step by step, persuade, teach, and catch
contradictions that show up as sentences that can't both be true. They tend to
excel where the problem *is* essentially linguistic or logical — law, argument,
writing, negotiation, the close reading of rules. Their characteristic failure
mode is fluency mistaken for understanding: a well-formed sentence can feel like a
solved problem even when nothing underneath it has actually been worked out. Words
are easy to arrange into something that *sounds* coherent but isn't.

The **abstract conceptual thinker** attacks the same problem by building a model.
They reach for the underlying structure — the relationships, the invariants, the
shape of the system — and manipulate that directly, often non-verbally, only
translating into words at the end if forced to. Their strength is seeing how
things connect: transferring a pattern from one domain to another, spotting that
two superficially different problems are "the same problem," grasping a system
whole. They tend to excel in mathematics, physics, engineering, design,
programming, and anywhere the essential difficulty is structural rather than
verbal. Their characteristic failure mode is the inverse of the verbal reasoner's:
they *know* something is true — they can see it — but struggle to render it into
the linear, explicit chain of sentences other people need in order to follow,
check, or be convinced. "It's obvious" is not an argument anyone else can use.

Put two such people in a room and the friction is predictable. The verbal reasoner
experiences the conceptual thinker as vague, hand-wavy, unable to "just explain
it." The conceptual thinker experiences the verbal reasoner as someone confidently
shuffling words around a problem they haven't actually modeled. Each is partly
right. Articulation without a model is empty; a model without articulation is
unsharable.

## What the evidence actually shows

It is worth being careful here, because this territory is littered with
pop-psychology myths — "left-brain versus right-brain" people, fixed "learning
styles" — that the research does not support. The real findings are more
interesting and more modest.

First, thought genuinely can be non-verbal, and language is not its precondition.
People in a *tip-of-the-tongue* state hold a specific concept clearly in mind
while the word for it refuses to come — proof that the concept and its label are
separable. Famous testimony points the same way: when the mathematician Jacques
Hadamard surveyed how mathematicians think, many reported reasoning in images and
"vague" non-verbal forms, putting things into words only afterward; Einstein
described his productive thinking as involving "signs and more or less clear
images" that were "visual and some of muscular type," with words sought only at a
later stage.

Second, the variation between people is real, not just stylistic flourish. Some
people have little or no inner monologue (sometimes called *anendophasia*), and
they reason perfectly well. Some people cannot voluntarily form mental images at
all (*aphantasia*) — yet many work successfully in visual and technical fields,
having simply built their conceptual thinking on non-pictorial structure. These
conditions sit at the ends of broad spectra along which everyone falls somewhere.

Third — and this is the most important corrective — almost nobody is purely one
type. The honest picture is not two tribes but a *profile*: each person has a
characteristic balance and a default they reach for first, while retaining access
to the other mode. Dual-coding accounts of cognition hold that we store and
process information in *both* a verbal and a non-verbal channel, and that the two
ordinarily work together. The strongest thinkers are usually those who move fluidly
between them — building a model conceptually, then stress-testing it by forcing it
into explicit words, then revising the model when the words won't come out clean.

## Why the distinction matters

If the categories were merely descriptive, they would be a curiosity. They matter
because they change how we should teach, collaborate, and judge our own
understanding.

In **collaboration**, knowing which mode a colleague favors tells you how to
exchange ideas with them: hand the verbal reasoner a written argument; hand the
conceptual thinker a diagram or a worked example and let them reconstruct the
argument themselves. In **teaching**, the goal is not to match a student's
supposed fixed "style" — that idea has been tested and largely failed — but to
ensure ideas are presented in *both* currencies, since an explanation that lands in
words for one student lands as a picture for another, and most ideas are
understood best when both are available.

Most personally, the distinction is a check on a private illusion. The verbal
thinker should be suspicious of fluency: the test of an idea is whether there is a
working model underneath the sentences, not whether the sentences sound right. The
conceptual thinker should be suspicious of "obviousness": an insight you cannot
render into words you cannot fully share, cannot easily check, and may not
understand as well as you feel you do. The act of translating between the two — of
forcing the wordless model into language, or of building a real model behind the
words — is not a chore that comes after thinking. Much of the time, it *is* the
thinking. We understand a thing best at exactly the point where we can hold it both
ways at once.
